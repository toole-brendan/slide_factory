# The core vocabulary

The terms used to describe United States defense contracting are widely misread because nearly every one of them belongs to a particular **dimension** — instrument, award structure, ordering, pricing, time, or performance chain — and answers only that dimension's question. The reliable way to misinterpret an award is to take a term from one dimension and treat it as though it answered another, for instance reading *IDIQ* (an instrument) as if it named a pricing type. This chapter is a glossary: it reproduces the core vocabulary in a single reference table and then expands, in short encyclopedic entries, the pairs that are confused most often. Deep treatment of each concept is deferred to the dedicated chapter, with cross-links provided. The dimensional framework these terms hang from is the subject of [The dimensions of a federal contract](01-dimensions-of-a-federal-contract.md).

## The core vocabulary table

The following table gives, for each term, what it actually describes, whether the term itself names a contract, and the misunderstanding that most often attaches to it. The column "Is it itself a contract?" is included because much of the confusion in this field comes from assuming that every named thing is a standalone prime contract; many are not.[^c02-far2101]

| Term | What it actually describes | Is it itself a contract? | Most common misunderstanding |
|---|---|---|---|
| Award | A broad term for the Government's selection and the resulting instrument; depending on context it may mean a prime contract, an IDV, an order, a grant, or a transaction. | Sometimes | Assuming every "award" is a standalone prime contract. |
| Contract action | An award, modification, order, option exercise, or other action affecting a contract. | Not necessarily a new contract | Treating every reported action as a new award. |
| Standalone (definitive) contract | A prime contract under which work is acquired directly, rather than principally through child orders. | Yes | Thinking "definitive" means the entire potential value was funded. |
| IDV (Indefinite Delivery Vehicle) | Principally a federal contract-data umbrella for parent instruments against which orders or purchases may be placed. | Sometimes | Treating IDV as synonymous with IDIQ. |
| IDIQ | An indefinite-quantity contract containing a binding minimum and a stated maximum, with work purchased through orders. | Yes | Treating it as a pricing type such as FFP or T&M. |
| MAC — multiple-award contract | A structure in which several sources receive parallel prime contracts for the same or similar scope. | Each award is a separate contract | Assuming all awardees jointly hold one contract or each owns the entire ceiling. |
| MAC — multi-agency contract | A contract established by one agency for use by multiple agencies. | Yes | Confusing it with "multiple-award contract." |
| Task order | An order for services under a parent contract. | Yes, within the parent framework | Treating it as merely an administrative request. |
| Delivery order | An order for supplies under a parent contract. | Yes, within the parent framework | Assuming "task order" and "delivery order" are different vehicle types. |
| Option | A unilateral Government right to buy additional quantity or extend the contract term. | No; it is a contractual right | Treating unexercised options as guaranteed work. |
| Subcontract | A contract under which a prime or higher-tier subcontractor purchases supplies or services needed for prime performance. | Yes, but not with the Government | Treating the subcontractor as a direct federal contractor. |
| Subaward | A transparency and assistance-program term; for procurement awards it usually refers to a reported first-tier subcontract. | Usually a subcontract | Assuming every commercial vendor purchase is a reported subaward. |
| MYP | Multiyear procurement or multiyear contracting: a funding and commitment method covering more than one program year. | Implemented through a contract | Treating it as an ordinary contract with annual options. |
| BPA | A repetitive-purchase arrangement whose precise legal effect depends on whether it is a FAR Part 13, Schedule, or other authorized BPA. | Often not by itself | Assuming all BPAs operate under the same rules. |
| BOA | A Basic Ordering Agreement establishing terms and pricing methods for future orders or contracts. | No | Treating the BOA ceiling as an awarded contract amount. |

## IDV versus IDIQ

An **Indefinite Delivery Vehicle (IDV)** is principally a federal contract-data umbrella: it is the category, used in award reporting, for parent instruments against which orders or purchases may later be placed. It belongs to the data-classification dimension. An **IDIQ** — an indefinite-delivery, indefinite-quantity contract — is one specific instrument that contains a binding minimum and a stated maximum, with work purchased through orders; it belongs to the instrument dimension. The misconception is to treat the two as synonyms. They are not: IDIQ is one species within the broader IDV genus. For federal reporting purposes an IDV may include indefinite-delivery contracts, Federal Supply Schedules, blanket purchase agreements, basic ordering agreements, and other agreements that contain ordering provisions, only some of which are IDIQ contracts.[^c02-idv] The full taxonomy is developed in [IDV, IDIQ, MAC, GWAC, and Schedules](07-idv-idiq-mac-gwac-schedules.md), and the ordering mechanics of indefinite-delivery contracts are covered in [Indefinite-delivery contracts](06-indefinite-delivery-contracts.md).

## The two meanings of MAC

The acronym **MAC** denotes two unrelated concepts that share three letters, and conflating them is a frequent source of error.

A **multiple-award contract** is an award-structure value: several sources receive parallel prime contracts for the same or similar scope, after which specific work is competed among the holders. The defining facts are that each award is a separate contract and that no holder owns the entire ceiling; the awardees do not jointly hold one instrument. The common misunderstanding is to imagine a single shared contract or to attribute the full vehicle ceiling to each company.

A **multi-agency contract** is a different thing entirely: a contract established by one agency for use by multiple agencies. It is a single contract whose distinguishing feature is who may order from it, not how many holders it has. The misconception here is simply to confuse it with "multiple-award contract." Both senses, together with government-wide acquisition contracts (GWACs) and Schedules, are distinguished in [IDV, IDIQ, MAC, GWAC, and Schedules](07-idv-idiq-mac-gwac-schedules.md).

## Task order versus delivery order

A **task order** is an order for services placed under a parent contract; a **delivery order** is an order for supplies placed under a parent contract. Both belong to the ordering dimension, and each is a binding contract within the parent framework even though neither is a separate prime contract with the Government. Two misconceptions recur. The first treats an order — particularly a task order — as a mere administrative request rather than the binding instrument it is. The second assumes that "task order" and "delivery order" name two different *vehicle types*; they do not. They are the same kind of thing distinguished only by what is being bought, services or supplies, under the same kind of parent vehicle. The placement, competition, and identification of orders are treated in [Indefinite-delivery contracts](06-indefinite-delivery-contracts.md), and how orders surface in reporting data is covered in [How awards appear in federal data](04-contract-data-systems.md).

## Option

An **option** is, by definition, a unilateral Government right to purchase additional supplies or services or to extend the term of the contract during a specified period.[^c02-option] It belongs to the time dimension and, crucially, it is not itself a contract but a contractual right held by the Government. The dominant misconception is to treat unexercised options as guaranteed or already-awarded work: an option represents potential, not commitment, and its value should not be counted as obligated until the Government actually exercises it. Because the right is the Government's alone, the contractor cannot compel its exercise. Options, including the distinction between parent-level and order-level options, are the subject of [Options and award terms](09-options-and-award-terms.md).

## MYP

**MYP** — multiyear procurement, or multiyear contracting — is a funding-and-commitment method that covers more than one program year, not a contract type. It is implemented through a contract but is not the contract itself, and it differs from an ordinary contract carrying annual options precisely in its commitment structure: a multiyear arrangement commits the Government across program years rather than re-deciding each year through a unilateral option. The misconception is to treat MYP as merely an ordinary contract with annual options. Multiyear procurement and its application to major weapon production are developed in [Multiyear procurement and major weapon production](11-multiyear-procurement-and-production.md), and its relationship to options specifically is discussed in [Options and award terms](09-options-and-award-terms.md).

## Subcontract versus subaward

A **subcontract** is a contract under which a prime contractor, or a higher-tier subcontractor, purchases supplies or services needed to perform the prime contract. It is a real contract, but not one with the Government; the relationship runs between private parties. The misconception is to treat the subcontractor as a direct federal contractor — it is not, and the Government generally has no privity of contract with it. This legal structure is the subject of [Prime–subcontractor structure, privity, and flowdowns](12-prime-contracting-subcontracting-teaming.md).

A **subaward** is a different kind of term, drawn from the transparency and federal-assistance vocabulary. For procurement awards it usually refers to a reported first-tier subcontract — that is, the subset of subcontracts that are captured under federal subaward-reporting requirements. The misconception is to assume that every commercial vendor purchase a prime makes is a reportable subaward; many ordinary vendor purchases are not. The reporting regime, including what counts as a first-tier subaward, is covered in [Subawards, subcontracts, and first-tier reporting](12-prime-contracting-subcontracting-teaming.md).

## BPA versus BOA

A **blanket purchase agreement (BPA)** is a repetitive-purchase arrangement, but its precise legal effect depends on the authority under which it is established — whether it is a FAR Part 13 (simplified acquisition) BPA, a Schedule BPA, or another authorized BPA. The misconception is to assume that all BPAs operate under the same rules; they do not, and the governing authority changes the analysis. Importantly, a BPA is often not, by itself, a contract.

A **basic ordering agreement (BOA)** is not a contract at all. It is an instrument that establishes terms and pricing methods for future orders or contracts; the actual contracts come into being only when orders are placed against it. The misconception is to read a BOA's stated ceiling as an awarded contract amount — it is neither awarded money nor an obligation. Both arrangements appear as IDVs in federal reporting and are addressed alongside the other "not a contract type" traps in [Common "not a contract type" traps](18-not-a-contract-type-traps.md), with the dollar-figure pitfalls covered in [Awards, ceilings, obligations, and spending](03-awards-ceilings-obligations-spending.md).

## How orders are identified in the data

Several of the terms above interact with how an award is identified in federal contract data, and a single rule prevents much confusion. For reporting, an IDV may include indefinite-delivery contracts, Schedules, BPAs, BOAs, and other agreements with ordering provisions. An order placed against one of these parents generally carries its own Procurement Instrument Identifier (PIID) while also identifying the parent instrument, so that the order is both a thing in its own right and a child of a named umbrella.[^c02-idv] The same vocabulary that distinguishes parent from child in prose — IDV versus order, definitive contract versus task or delivery order — is what the data systems use to keep awards, ceilings, and obligations from being double-counted. The reporting structure is developed in [How awards appear in federal data](04-contract-data-systems.md).

## The throughline

Every term in this glossary belongs to a dimension, as set out in [The dimensions of a federal contract](01-dimensions-of-a-federal-contract.md). The fastest way to misread an award is to treat a term from one dimension — for example *IDIQ*, which names an instrument — as if it answered another dimension's question, such as how the contractor is paid. That is why this reference repeatedly separates the instrument question from the pricing question, developed in [Pricing types and cost risk](08-pricing-types-and-cost-risk.md); the ceiling question from the obligation question, developed in [Awards, ceilings, obligations, and spending](03-awards-ceilings-obligations-spending.md); and the award-action question from the modification question, developed in [Contract modifications](14-contract-modifications.md). A term that is correctly assigned to its dimension is rarely misread; a term that is borrowed across dimensions almost always is.
