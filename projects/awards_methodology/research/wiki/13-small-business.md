# Small business in defense contracting

In the **competition** and **performance-chain** dimensions introduced in [The dimensions of a federal contract](01-dimensions-of-a-federal-contract.md), "small business" is not a fixed attribute that a company carries from one award to the next. A firm is small or large for a *particular procurement* and its *assigned North American Industry Classification System (NAICS) code*, so the same company can be small for one acquisition and large for the next without anything about the company changing. This chapter sets out the per-procurement size-determination sequence, reproduces the principal socioeconomic categories, explains the small-business "rule of two" and the current dollar thresholds, describes the ways small-business participation occurs on multiple-award contracts, catalogs the recurring traps (affiliation, the ostensible subcontractor, limitations on subcontracting, the nonmanufacturer rule, recertification, and the prime-versus-subcontract-credit confusion), and closes on what the phrase "small-business dominance" of a defense sector does and does not mean.

## Size is determined per procurement and per NAICS

A company does not hold a single, portable "small" status. Size is assigned acquisition by acquisition through a sequence that begins with the contracting officer and ends with potential later challenge.

1. The contracting officer assigns a **NAICS code** based on the *principal purpose* of the acquisition.
2. The Small Business Administration (SBA) associates that NAICS code with a **size standard**.
3. The standard is normally expressed as **average annual receipts** or **number of employees**.
4. The company must generally include relevant **affiliates** in its size calculation.
5. The company **represents or certifies** its status.
6. Size and socioeconomic status can later be **challenged or require recertification**.

The measurement windows are specific. For receipts-based standards, SBA generally uses the latest **five complete fiscal years**; for employee-based standards, the average number of employees over the latest **24 calendar months**.[^c13-sizestds] Because each NAICS code carries its own threshold, the consequences for a single firm can look contradictory until the per-procurement rule is applied. The same contractor might be small for a receipts-based consulting NAICS, large for another NAICS with a lower receipts threshold, small when it wins a parent multiple-award contract (MAC), no longer small after an acquisition several years later, or required to recertify for a particular order. None of those outcomes conflicts with the others, because each is a statement about a different procurement, NAICS code, or point in time, in the same way that the dimensional model treats an award's other attributes as independent axes.

## The socioeconomic categories

Beyond the baseline small-business size determination, federal contracting recognizes a set of **socioeconomic categories** that support set-asides and agency goaling. Each has its own core qualification, and several are SBA-certified statuses rather than mere self-representations.

| Category | Core qualification |
|---|---|
| Small business | Meets the size standard for the assigned NAICS, including applicable affiliates |
| 8(a) participant | SBA-certified small business generally owned and controlled at least 51% by qualifying socially and economically disadvantaged U.S. citizens, with financial and program eligibility requirements |
| Small Disadvantaged Business (SDB) | A disadvantaged-business status used for federal contracting and goaling; related to, but not identical with, 8(a) program participation |
| HUBZone | SBA-certified small business with eligible ownership, principal office in a HUBZone, and generally at least 35% of employees residing in HUBZones |
| WOSB (woman-owned small business) | SBA-certified small business at least 51% owned and controlled by U.S.-citizen women, with women managing daily and long-term decisions |
| EDWOSB (economically disadvantaged WOSB) | WOSB meeting additional economic-disadvantage limits |
| SDVOSB (service-disabled veteran-owned small business) | SBA-certified small business at least 51% owned and controlled by one or more qualifying service-disabled veterans |
| VOSB (veteran-owned small business) | Veteran-owned status particularly relevant to U.S. Department of Veterans Affairs (VA) contracting; SDVOSB has broader governmentwide set-aside authority |

SBA's current published criteria include the 51% ownership and control requirements, the HUBZone principal-office and employee-residency tests, and the applicable individual financial limits for 8(a) and EDWOSB eligibility.[^c13-8a]

A version caveat applies to the 8(a) program specifically. As of **June 25, 2026**, SBA has issued a **proposed — not final — 8(a) rule** that would require individually owned applicants to establish social disadvantage through fact-based evidence rather than a racial presumption.[^c13-8areform] The distinction between the current regulation (which retains the presumption) and the proposal (which would replace it) should be read as the difference between codified rule and pending change, in the same way the reference elsewhere distinguishes codified FAR from the DoD class deviation.

## The rule of two, the thresholds, and MAC participation modes

The principal mechanism that channels work to small businesses is the small-business **rule of two**. In simplified terms, above the micro-purchase threshold and through the simplified acquisition threshold, acquisitions are generally set aside for small businesses unless the contracting officer does not reasonably expect competitive offers from at least **two responsible small businesses** at fair-market prices, quality, and delivery. Above the simplified acquisition threshold, a **total small-business set-aside** is generally made when there is a reasonable expectation of at least two responsible small-business offers and award at a fair market price, under FAR 19.502-2.[^c13-ruleoftwo]

The dollar boundaries that frame the rule are dated facts that change on their own cycle. The current general thresholds, effective **October 1, 2025**, are a **micro-purchase threshold of $15,000** and a **simplified acquisition threshold of $350,000**.[^c13-thresholds] These are the general figures only; there are exceptions, specialized thresholds, and different procedures for contingencies, overseas buying, humanitarian operations, and particular statutory programs.

On multiple-award contracts, small-business participation is not a single switch but one of several structural modes, several of which can be combined on one vehicle. Participation can occur through:

```text
Small-business participation on a multiple-award contract
│
├── An entirely small-business MAC
├── A partial set-aside
├── A small-business reserve
├── Separate socioeconomic pools (e.g., 8(a), HUBZone, SDVOSB, WOSB)
├── An order-level set-aside
└── Unrestricted orders for which small and large holders compete
```

These modes connect directly to the ordering mechanics in [Indefinite-delivery contracts](06-indefinite-delivery-contracts.md): whether a small firm sees work under a MAC can depend on whether the vehicle is set aside, reserved, or pooled, and on whether a given order is itself set aside or competed unrestricted among all holders.

## Small-business traps

A set of recurring structural traps determines whether a nominally small firm is in fact eligible, and whether the work it performs counts the way the parties assume. Each is a distinct rule.

### Affiliation

SBA may aggregate the size of companies under **common ownership, control, management, or other affiliation rules** under 13 C.F.R. § 121.103, so that a nominally small entity is treated as large once its affiliates are counted.[^c13-affiliation] Affiliation is the reason size is computed at the level of a corporate family rather than a single legal entity, and it is the most common way a firm that "feels" small fails the size standard.

### Ostensible subcontractor

A small prime may lose eligibility under the **ostensible subcontractor** rule if a non-similarly-situated subcontractor performs the *primary and vital* requirements of the contract or if the prime is *unusually reliant* on that subcontractor. The rule does not forbid using subcontractors to strengthen a proposal — SBA permits a subcontractor's past performance and experience to be cited — but it targets excessive reliance or the effective transfer of the core requirement to a firm that is not itself small.

### Limitations on subcontracting

For covered set-asides, the prime generally may not pay **non-similarly-situated subcontractors** more than the applicable share of the amount paid by the Government, under FAR 52.219-14: **50% for services**; **50% for supplies** (excluding specified material costs); **85% for general construction** (excluding materials); or **75% for specialty-trade construction** (excluding materials).[^c13-limsubk] A subcontract to a **similarly situated entity** can receive more favorable treatment — generally meaning the subcontractor is itself small under the applicable NAICS and possesses the same required socioeconomic status. Limitations on subcontracting are one of the four distinct subcontracting-compliance systems distinguished in [Prime–subcontractor structure, privity, and flowdowns](12-prime-contracting-subcontracting-teaming.md); they constrain how much of the *prime's own* work may flow outward, which is a separate question from subcontracting-plan goals or first-tier transparency reporting.

### Nonmanufacturer rule

For a **supply set-aside**, a small reseller ordinarily must provide a product manufactured by a **qualifying small domestic manufacturer** unless SBA has issued an applicable waiver or another exception applies — the **nonmanufacturer rule**.[^c13-nonmfr] The rule prevents a supply set-aside from being satisfied by reselling a large or foreign manufacturer's product without an authorized exception.

### Recertification

A firm's eligibility — and whether the agency may **count the award toward small-business goals** — can diverge from its original status after a merger, acquisition, long-term contract milestone, option, or order-level recertification request. Under current SBA rules at 13 C.F.R. § 125.12, an entity may remain eligible for some unrestricted orders while becoming ineligible for a particular set-aside order or no longer countable toward agency goals.[^c13-recert] Recertification is therefore the point at which eligibility and goal-credit, normally aligned, come apart; it is closely related to the option-exercise and long-term-vehicle mechanics treated in [Subawards, subcontracts, and first-tier reporting](12-prime-contracting-subcontracting-teaming.md) and in [Single-award, single-source, and sole-source](16-single-award-and-sole-source.md).

### Prime award versus subcontract credit

A large-business prime award does **not** become a small-business prime award merely because a portion of the work — say, 40% — is subcontracted to small firms. **Prime-contract small-business dollars** and **small-business subcontracting dollars** are separate measurements that are tallied and goaled independently. Conflating them is the error behind many claims that a particular large award "is" a small-business award; the distinction parallels the broader caution in [Common "not a contract type" traps](18-not-a-contract-type-traps.md) against reading one dimension's label as if it answered a different dimension's question.

## What "small-business dominance" of a sector does and does not mean

Whether small businesses "dominate" a defense sector depends entirely on the **denominator**, and at least four different measurements are in circulation:

```text
Four denominators for "small-business share"
│
├── (1) Percentage of award actions
├── (2) Percentage of prime-contract obligations
├── (3) Percentage of subcontract dollars
└── (4) Percentage within a particular NAICS or PSC
```

Small businesses do **not** dominate total DoD prime-contract dollars. DoD's **FY2025 goals** were **23.17% for prime-contracting dollars** and **30% for subcontracting dollars** — goals expressed as minority shares of the whole, not majorities.[^c13-dodgoals]

The most-cited sector figures must be read with their denominator attached. GAO found that **professional, scientific, and technical services; manufacturing; and construction** together accounted for **77% of all DoD obligations to small businesses in FY2020**; within professional services, the major categories included architectural and engineering services, computer systems design, and scientific research and development.[^c13-gao104621] That figure does *not* mean small businesses received 77% of all DoD spending in those sectors. It means those sectors received 77% of the dollars DoD awarded *to small businesses* — a share of the small-business total, not a share of the sector. For a defined group of DoD service contracts, GAO separately found that about **27% of obligations from FY2017 through FY2022** went to small businesses.[^c13-gao106123]

Read with the denominators kept straight, the practical picture is consistent. Small firms tend to have substantial opportunities in construction and installation repair; architecture and engineering; information-technology and cybersecurity support; scientific and technical services; research, development, and prototyping; training; base operations; maintenance and logistics; niche components and manufacturing; and staff augmentation and professional support. Large companies generally dominate the **prime-integration role** for major aircraft, ships, missiles, nuclear systems, and satellites, while small businesses frequently participate *underneath* those awards as suppliers and subcontractors — the performance-chain pattern described in [Prime–subcontractor structure, privity, and flowdowns](12-prime-contracting-subcontracting-teaming.md). GAO has likewise observed that major acquisition programs typically award to large businesses and often operate with limited competition. "Small-business dominance," then, is rarely a claim about who receives the most defense dollars in a sector; it is usually a claim about where, within the smaller pool of dollars reserved for or won by small firms, those dollars concentrate.
