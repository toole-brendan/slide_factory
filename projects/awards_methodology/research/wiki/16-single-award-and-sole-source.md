# Single-award, single-source, and sole-source

Three terms that sound interchangeable describe entirely different things, and confusing them is one of the most common errors in reading defense contract awards. The terms **single-award** and **multiple-award** describe the *number* of holders a vehicle has; the term **sole-source** describes the *absence* of competition. These are independent dimensions in the sense developed in [The dimensions of a federal contract](01-dimensions-of-a-federal-contract.md): the award-structure dimension answers "how many companies hold the vehicle," while the competition dimension answers "who was allowed to compete." Because the two questions are orthogonal, a one-holder vehicle can be the product of vigorous competition, and a fully competed multiple-award vehicle can still produce an order directed to a single holder. This chapter separates the concepts, sets out the four combinations they generate, recommends a disciplined vocabulary, and states the two regulatory rules that attach to single-award and noncompetitive actions: the preference for multiple awards and the written determination required for a single-award task- or delivery-order vehicle over $150 million.

## Two independent dimensions

The whole subject reduces to keeping two axes apart. The **award-structure** axis counts holders: a **single-award IDIQ** is held by one prime, while a **multiple-award IDIQ** is held by several primes who hold parallel contracts for the same scope, as developed in [Indefinite-delivery contracts](06-indefinite-delivery-contracts.md). The **competition** axis describes how the award was reached: a **competed** award results from full and open competition (or competition within a set-aside pool), while a **sole-source** award is negotiated with one contractor without full and open competition, under a statutory authority or authorized exception. Neither axis determines the other. The number of holders a vehicle ends up with says nothing about whether the public was invited to compete for it, and the presence or absence of competition says nothing about how many holders eventually result.

A further term, **single-source**, sits awkwardly between the two and is best avoided. The FAR sometimes uses "single source" when discussing large single-award vehicles, but the phrase reads as though it were a synonym for "sole-source," which it is not. Because *single-source* invites exactly the collapse this chapter warns against, the unambiguous label for a one-holder vehicle is **single-award IDIQ**.

## The four cases

Once the two axes are held apart, four cases follow, and each has a different correct description. The structured illustrations below show how the same word — "one winner," "several winners" — can sit on either side of the competition line.

A **competed single-award IDIQ** is reached through full and open competition that produces one winner. The competition was real; the result is simply that a single firm holds the vehicle.

```text
Competed single-award IDIQ
│
├── Full and open competition  (ten offerors compete)
│
└── One winner selected
        │
        └── Single-award IDIQ to the one winner
              (competed, NOT sole-source)
```

A **sole-source single-award IDIQ** is negotiated with one contractor without full and open competition. Here the vehicle is both single-award (one holder) and sole-source (no competition), so both labels are accurate at once.

```text
Sole-source single-award IDIQ
│
├── No full and open competition
│      └── Justification prepared (only Company A can perform)
│
└── Single-award IDIQ to Company A
      (single-award AND sole-source)
```

A **multiple-award IDIQ** gives several companies parallel contracts for the same scope; those holders ordinarily receive a fair opportunity to compete for orders, as described in [Indefinite-delivery contracts](06-indefinite-delivery-contracts.md).

```text
Competed multiple-award IDIQ
│
├── Full and open competition
│
└── Several winners (holders A, B, C, D)
        │
        └── Orders competed among holders under fair opportunity
```

A **sole-source order under a multiple-award IDIQ** is the case that most often confuses readers, because the parent vehicle and the order sit on opposite sides of the competition line. The parent may have been fully competed, yet a particular task order is directed to one holder under an exception to fair opportunity — for example, a logical follow-on order under the exception covered in [Indefinite-delivery contracts](06-indefinite-delivery-contracts.md). Current FAR terminology expressly refers to an order placed with one holder under a fair-opportunity exception as an order placed on a **"sole-source basis."**[^c16-soleorder]

```text
Sole-source order under a multiple-award IDIQ
│
├── Parent MAC fully competed  (holders A, B, C, D)
│
└── Task order directed to holder C
      under an exception to fair opportunity
      = order placed on a "sole-source basis"
      (parent competed; order not competed)
```

## The four combinations in one table

The same four cases laid side by side make the independence of the axes explicit. The vehicle structure (the holder count) and the parent competition (whether the public competed) vary separately, and only their combination yields the correct description.

| Vehicle structure | Parent competition | Correct description |
|---|---|---|
| One holder | Several companies competed | Competed single-award IDIQ |
| One holder | No full competition | Sole-source single-award IDIQ |
| Several holders | Several companies competed | Competed multiple-award IDIQ |
| Several holders, but one receives a particular order without order competition | Parent competed; order uses an exception | Sole-source order under a MAC |

The first two rows share a holder count but differ in competition; the last two rows share several holders but differ in whether the contested action is the parent award or a single order beneath it. Reading the table by column rather than by row is the quickest way to internalize that "one holder" and "no competition" are simply not the same fact.

## Recommended terminology reservations

Because each of these words belongs to a single dimension, the clearest usage reserves each term for the question it actually answers. Four reservations keep the vocabulary disciplined:

- **Single-award** and **multiple-award** are reserved for the *number of vehicle holders* — an award-structure fact.
- **Sole-source** is reserved for the *absence of competition* — a competition fact, equally applicable to a whole vehicle or to a single order placed on a sole-source basis.
- **IDIQ** is reserved for the *delivery and ordering structure* — the instrument, as set out in [IDV, IDIQ, MAC, GWAC, and Schedules](07-idv-idiq-mac-gwac-schedules.md).
- **FFP, FPI, CPFF**, and the other pricing labels are reserved for the *pricing arrangement* — how the contractor is paid and who bears cost risk, the subject of [Pricing types and cost risk](08-pricing-types-and-cost-risk.md).

Held to these reservations, a description such as "a competed single-award firm-fixed-price IDIQ" parses cleanly: competed names the competition, single-award the holder count, firm-fixed-price the pricing, and IDIQ the instrument, with no term doing two jobs. The term **single-source** is set aside entirely, because its resemblance to "sole-source" makes it ambiguous; *single-award IDIQ* says the same thing without inviting the error. The same discipline underlies the warning in [Common "not a contract type" traps](18-not-a-contract-type-traps.md) that "sole source" names a competition posture rather than a kind of contract.

## The multiple-award preference and the $150 million determination

Two regulatory rules attach specifically to these distinctions, and both belong to the competition and award-structure dimensions rather than to the instrument or pricing dimensions.

First, the current FAR Part 16, as rewritten under the Revolutionary FAR Overhaul, **prefers multiple awards** when making multiple awards is practicable.[^c16-preference] The preference is a default toward distributing a vehicle among several competing holders rather than concentrating it in one; it operates on the award-structure axis and is independent of how any individual order is later priced or competed.

Second, a task- or delivery-order contract over **$150 million** — including all options — that is awarded to a **single source** requires a specific written **determination** before award.[^c16-determination] This determination is a creature of the award-structure dimension: it is triggered by the choice to use one holder for a high-value ordering vehicle, not by any lack of competition in reaching that holder. It is therefore **separate from, and additional to**, any FAR Part 6 justification that a noncompetitive award would independently require. A single-award vehicle over the threshold needs the written determination even if it was fully and openly competed; a sole-source vehicle below the threshold needs the FAR Part 6 justification but not this determination; and a high-value sole-source single-award vehicle needs both. As of the article date, June 25, 2026, DoD contracting officers apply the overhauled Part 16 — including the multiple-award preference and the single-source determination requirement — under a class deviation effective March 16, 2026.[^c16-deviation]

## The FAR Part 6 basis for a noncompetitive award

When an award is in fact made without full and open competition, the competition dimension requires its own justification under FAR Part 6, which is distinct from the award-structure determination just described. The most common basis is that **only one responsible source** — or, for the Department of Defense under a class deviation, **one or a limited number of responsible sources** — can satisfy the agency's requirement, the authority at FAR 6.302-1.[^c16-far6302] This basis explains many sole-source actions in defense, where the practical impossibility of qualifying an additional source quickly, or a deliberately limited industrial base, leaves one or a few firms able to perform; the *Virginia*-class submarine program treated in [Multiyear procurement and major weapon production](11-multiyear-procurement-and-production.md) is a worked example of a limited-industrial-base judgment that is distinct from any one-holder vehicle.

The justification for a sole-source *award* under FAR 6.302-1 should not be confused with the **justification for an exception to fair opportunity** that supports a sole-source *order* under a multiple-award vehicle, the document described in [Indefinite-delivery contracts](06-indefinite-delivery-contracts.md). The first departs from full and open competition for an award; the second departs from fair opportunity among existing holders for an order. Both produce something correctly called a sole-source action, but they live at different levels of the structure — one at the parent vehicle, one at the order — which is exactly why the holder-count and competition axes must be tracked separately. The same separation matters for small-business set-asides and recertification, where a single holder's eligibility can shift over a vehicle's life, as developed in [Small business in defense contracting](13-small-business.md).
