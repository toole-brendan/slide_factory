# Pricing, commerciality, and cost risk

**Pricing type** is the dimension of a federal award that answers how the contractor is paid and which party bears the financial consequence when the actual cost of performance differs from what was expected. It is a separate axis from the **instrument** dimension, which governs how work is accessed: an [indefinite-delivery indefinite-quantity (IDIQ)](06-indefinite-delivery-contracts.md) contract, a [multiple-award contract](07-idv-idiq-mac-gwac-schedules.md), an individual task order, a standalone (definitive) contract, or a [multiyear contract](11-multiyear-procurement-and-production.md) may each use one — or several — of the pricing arrangements described in this chapter. The arrangements group into four families: fixed-price, incentive, cost-reimbursement, and time-and-materials. Each family allocates **cost risk** differently, and the choice of family is governed by FAR Part 16 (applied by DoD under the current Revolutionary FAR Overhaul class deviation), not by the choice of vehicle.[^c08-deviation]

## The vehicle-versus-pricing rule

The compact rule that organizes this chapter is that **vehicle type determines how work is accessed, while pricing type determines how the contractor is paid and who bears cost risk**. These are independent axes. An IDIQ may issue firm-fixed-price orders, cost-reimbursement orders, or both; a standalone contract or a multiyear contract may likewise draw on any pricing family. Nothing about the ordering structure constrains the payment arrangement, and nothing about the payment arrangement implies a particular ordering structure. This independence is the same point made from the instrument side in [The dimensions of a federal contract](01-dimensions-of-a-federal-contract.md), where treating IDIQ as though it were itself a pricing type is identified as one of the most common reading errors. A reader who keeps the two axes separate can correctly describe an award as, for example, "a multiple-award IDIQ issuing fixed-price-incentive task orders," with no contradiction between the labels.

## The pricing families

The following table sets out the principal pricing arrangements, the basic mechanism of each, and the party that bears the principal cost risk under it. The arrangements are grouped into the fixed-price, cost-reimbursement, and time-and-materials families, with the incentive constructs appearing in both the fixed-price and cost-reimbursement groups.

| Pricing family | Basic mechanism | Principal cost risk |
|---|---|---|
| Firm-fixed-price (FFP) | Contractor delivers the specified result for an agreed price | Mostly contractor |
| Fixed-price with economic price adjustment (FP-EPA) | Fixed price adjusts under specified indices or contingencies | Shared for defined economic changes |
| Fixed-price incentive (FPI) | Target cost and profit, sharing formula, and ceiling determine the final price | Shared until the ceiling; contractor above the ceiling |
| Cost-reimbursement | Government reimburses allowable incurred costs up to the applicable estimate or funding limit | Mostly Government |
| Cost-plus-fixed-fee (CPFF) | Allowable costs plus a fixed fee | Mostly Government; fee normally fixed |
| Cost-plus-incentive-fee (CPIF) | Allowable costs plus a fee adjusted by an objective formula | Shared through the incentive formula |
| Cost-plus-award-fee (CPAF) | Allowable costs plus a discretionary award fee based on performance | Mostly Government, with a performance incentive |
| Time-and-materials (T&M) | Fixed hourly labor rates plus actual material costs | Significant Government risk |
| Labor-hour | T&M without contractor-furnished materials | Significant Government risk |
| Hybrid | Different CLINs or orders use different pricing types | Depends on each element |

### The fixed-price family

In a **firm-fixed-price (FFP)** arrangement the contractor agrees to deliver the specified result for an agreed price that is not adjusted on the basis of the contractor's incurred cost. The contractor therefore bears most of the cost risk: if performance costs more than estimated, the contractor absorbs the difference, and if it costs less, the contractor keeps the difference. FFP places the maximum risk and the full responsibility for all costs and resulting profit (or loss) on the contractor, which is what makes it the default arrangement when the work and its cost are well understood.

A **fixed-price contract with economic price adjustment (FP-EPA)** is a fixed-price arrangement in which the otherwise-firm price may move up or down under defined indices or contingencies — for example, changes in established prices for specified materials or in published labor or commodity indices. The adjustment mechanism is limited to the economic changes the contract names, so cost risk is *shared for those defined economic changes* while the contractor continues to bear the ordinary performance risk.

A **fixed-price incentive (FPI)** contract sets a target cost, a target profit, a sharing formula, and a ceiling price. Actual cost is compared against the target cost, and the difference is divided between the parties according to the sharing formula, which adjusts the contractor's profit; the final price the Government pays is determined by that calculation but cannot exceed the ceiling price. Cost risk is therefore *shared until the ceiling is reached*, after which the contractor bears additional cost alone. The same family includes **price redetermination**, a fixed-price mechanism under which the price is established or revised at stated points (for example, after an initial period of performance), used when a firm price cannot be set for the entire effort at the outset; it is mentioned here only to place it within the family.

### The cost-reimbursement family

Under a **cost-reimbursement** arrangement the Government reimburses the contractor's allowable incurred costs up to the applicable estimate or funding limit, rather than paying a price fixed in advance. Because payment follows actual allowable cost, the Government bears most of the cost risk; the contractor's exposure is principally to the fee it earns rather than to the costs it incurs, subject to the estimated cost and funding ceilings established in the contract. The family is distinguished internally by how the fee is determined.

- **Cost-plus-fixed-fee (CPFF)** pays allowable costs plus a fee that is fixed in dollar terms at award. The fee does not vary with actual cost, so the Government bears most of the cost risk and the fee is normally fixed regardless of performance outcome.
- **Cost-plus-incentive-fee (CPIF)** pays allowable costs plus a fee that is adjusted by an objective formula tied to the relationship between actual cost (or other measured outcomes) and a target. Cost risk is *shared through the incentive formula*, which raises the fee for performance better than target and lowers it for performance worse than target, within stated minimum and maximum fee limits.
- **Cost-plus-award-fee (CPAF)** pays allowable costs plus a discretionary award fee determined by the Government's evaluation of the contractor's performance against stated criteria. The Government still bears most of the cost risk, but the award fee supplies a performance incentive through a subjective, periodically determined judgment rather than a formula.

### Time-and-materials and labor-hour

A **time-and-materials (T&M)** arrangement pays the contractor at fixed hourly labor rates for hours worked, plus the actual cost of materials. Because the contractor is paid for the hours it expends rather than for a defined result, T&M carries significant Government risk: it provides no positive profit incentive to control hours, so it requires appropriate Government surveillance to ensure efficient performance. A **labor-hour** contract is a variant of T&M used where the contractor furnishes no materials — that is, T&M without the materials component — and it allocates cost risk the same way.

### Hybrid pricing

A **hybrid** arrangement uses different pricing types for different elements of the same award, so that the cost-risk allocation *depends on each element*. Hybrid pricing is common: a single task order can carry a firm-fixed-price contract line-item number (CLIN) alongside a cost-reimbursement CLIN, with each line item priced under the family appropriate to its portion of the work. When a contract or order mixes arrangements in this way, the pricing type is identified at the line-item level rather than for the award as a whole, which is why contract line-item structure is the unit at which payment terms and funding are actually read. The mechanics of identifying pricing and funding line by line are developed in [CLINs, SLINs, and funding traceability](15-clins-slins-and-funding-traceability.md).

## Part 16 selection guidance

FAR Part 16 expresses the choice among pricing types as a function of how confidently the work and its cost can be estimated, with cost risk shifting toward the Government as that confidence declines. Under the current text, the guidance directs:

- **Firm-fixed-price** when risk is minimal or can be predicted with reasonable certainty — that is, when the requirement and its cost are well enough defined that the contractor can responsibly accept a fixed price;
- **Cost-reimbursement** when requirements or costs cannot be estimated with sufficient accuracy to use a fixed price, so that fixing a price in advance would force the contractor either to inflate the price against uncertainty or to accept indeterminate risk; and
- **Time-and-materials** only when the extent or duration of the work cannot be estimated with reasonable confidence at the time of award, and even then only with additional controls.

Because T&M provides no built-in incentive to control hours, FAR Part 16 attaches two further conditions to its use: the contract must include a **ceiling price** that the contractor exceeds at its own risk, and the Government must provide **heightened surveillance** of performance to give reasonable assurance that efficient methods and effective cost controls are being used.[^c08-part16] The labor-hour variant carries the same conditions. These requirements make T&M and labor-hour the arrangements of last resort within the selection hierarchy rather than ordinary choices.

## Performance-incentive features alongside pricing

Several constructs that adjust a contractor's compensation for performance sit *alongside* the pricing type rather than constituting pricing types of their own. **Incentive fee** (as in the fixed-price-incentive and cost-plus-incentive-fee arrangements) and **award fee** (as in cost-plus-award-fee) are the fee-determination mechanisms described above; they modify how much the contractor earns on top of, or as part of, the underlying fixed-price or cost-reimbursement structure. A related construct, the **award term**, is not a fee at all but an additional period of performance that the contractor earns by meeting stated performance standards. An award term is therefore a performance-incentive feature expressed in time rather than money, and because it is a separately earned performance period it is treated with [options and award terms](09-options-and-award-terms.md) rather than as a pricing family. The distinction matters for reading an award correctly: a CPAF order and an order with an award term may both reward good performance, but the first does so through fee and the second through additional ordering time.

## How a price is substantiated: an orientation

The sections that follow are **orientation-level**, not an accounting manual: they sketch the machinery that surrounds the pricing arrangements above without attempting the detail an estimator or auditor would need. The unifying theme is the **business-system infrastructure that a given pricing arrangement obliges a contractor to maintain**. A pricing type does more than allocate cost risk; it dictates what a contractor must be able to substantiate, what records it must keep, and which audits and government reviews it must be prepared to pass. A firm-fixed-price commercial buy and a cost-reimbursement research contract sit at opposite ends of that obligation, and the distance between them is measured in business systems rather than in the price itself.

## Price analysis versus cost analysis

The two principal methods of evaluating a proposed price differ in what they look at. **Price analysis** evaluates a proposed price against the market — competing quotes, published catalogs, prior prices, or other comparison points — *without examining the individual cost elements* that make up the price. **Cost analysis** examines the build-up of estimated costs: direct labor, materials, indirect rates, and profit, element by element. Cost analysis applies precisely when adequate price competition or commerciality is absent, so that the market cannot by itself establish that a price is fair and reasonable. The choice of method under FAR 15.404-1 therefore determines what a contractor must substantiate: a price the market validates, or a cost structure the contractor must expose and defend.[^c08-far15404]

## Commercial-item pricing

Commercial products and services — bought under FAR Part 12 and defined at FAR 2.101 — are acquired on the basis of **price reasonableness** established from commercial and market pricing, generally **without certified cost or pricing data** under FAR 15.403-1. The commercial classification thus carries a direct pricing consequence: it routes the buy toward price analysis against the marketplace and away from the cost-build-up substantiation that noncommercial work attracts. This is why the commercial classification matters to pricing even though "commercial" is a *classification*, not a pricing type — a distinction catalogued among the cross-dimensional confusions in [Common "not a contract type" traps](18-not-a-contract-type-traps.md).[^c08-far12]

## Indirect rates and wrap rates

A contractor's price is not built from direct labor alone. It builds from **direct labor plus indirect rates** — overhead, general and administrative (G&A), and fringe — which compound on top of the base labor cost into an effective **wrap rate**, the multiplier that turns a raw labor dollar into a billed dollar. The decisive orientation point is that the wrap rate is an artifact of the **contractor's own cost structure**, not of the contract: two firms bidding the same labor can carry very different wrap rates, and the same firm carries its wrap rate across the contracts it holds. The composition and administration of these indirect rates are governed by FAR 31.203 and the indirect-rate administration of FAR Part 42.[^c08-indirect]

## Why cost-type work demands dependable timekeeping

Cost-reimbursement and time-and-materials or labor-hour arrangements reimburse **incurred labor**, which means the bill is only as trustworthy as the records of hours and costs behind it. Before a contractor can responsibly hold cost-type work, it must therefore maintain **auditable timekeeping** and an **adequate accounting system** that accumulates costs by contract and by CLIN — the criteria reflected in DFARS 252.242-7006 (Accounting System Administration) and the SF 1408 adequacy standard.[^c08-acctgsystem] This is the clearest illustration of the chapter's theme that the pricing arrangement drives the required infrastructure: the same business system that is optional background for a firm-fixed-price commercial buy becomes a precondition for cost-type award. The CLIN-level cost accumulation it depends on is developed in [CLINs, SLINs, and funding traceability](15-clins-slins-and-funding-traceability.md), and the invoicing that flows from it connects to the modification and invoicing mechanics in [Contract modifications](14-contract-modifications.md).

## Certified (truthful) cost or pricing data

Above the applicable threshold and absent an exception, an offeror must **submit and certify** cost or pricing data as accurate, complete, and current — the requirement long known as the Truth in Negotiations Act and now expressed as "truthful cost or pricing data." The certification is consequential because it shifts risk onto the contractor after award: if the certified data was **defective** — inaccurate, incomplete, or out of date as of the agreed date — the Government may seek a **price reduction** in the amount by which the defect inflated the price, under FAR 15.403-4.[^c08-tina] Commerciality and adequate price competition are among the exceptions that remove the requirement, which is one reason the commercial classification and the competition posture matter so directly to a contractor's substantiation burden.

## Cost Accounting Standards, DCAA, and contractor business systems

Three further pieces of infrastructure round out the orientation. The **Cost Accounting Standards (CAS)** — found in FAR Part 30 and codified at 48 C.F.R. Chapter 99 — govern how a covered contractor measures, assigns, and allocates costs, so that costs are accounted for consistently across its contracts. The **Defense Contract Audit Agency (DCAA)** performs the cost audits that test those measurements and the incurred costs a contractor bills. And DoD reviews a defined set of **contractor business systems** — accounting, estimating, purchasing, and others — for adequacy.[^c08-cas] These are the standing institutions behind the contract-specific obligations described above: CAS sets the measurement rules, DCAA audits against them, and the business-system reviews certify that the contractor's machinery is sound. The **purchasing-system** review in particular reaches the contractor's management of its own subcontracts, the subject of [Prime–subcontractor structure, privity, and flowdowns](12-prime-contracting-subcontracting-teaming.md).

## Reading pricing across vehicles

Keeping the pricing axis distinct from the instrument axis resolves several recurring ambiguities in contract announcements and federal data. A description such as "multiple-award IDIQ" says nothing about how any order under it is priced; the orders may be FFP, cost-reimbursement, T&M, or hybrid, and different orders under the same parent may differ. Conversely, "firm-fixed-price" says nothing about the ordering structure — an FFP award may be a single standalone contract, an order under an IDIQ, or a [multiyear](11-multiyear-procurement-and-production.md) production contract. Even a [single-award or sole-source](16-single-award-and-sole-source.md) procurement, which is a fact about the number of holders and the presence of competition, leaves the pricing type entirely open. The pricing family of a major weapon-production contract is similarly a separate question from whether it is competed, single-award, or multiyear; the funding-and-commitment method of [multiyear procurement](11-multiyear-procurement-and-production.md) can be paired with a fixed-price-incentive or other pricing arrangement as the program requires. In every case the reliable reading is to identify the instrument, the award structure, and the pricing type as three separate answers, and — where a contract mixes pricing arrangements — to descend to the CLIN level, because that is where the pricing type and the cost-risk allocation are actually fixed.
