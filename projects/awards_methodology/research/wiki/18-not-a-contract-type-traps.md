# Alternative acquisition pathways and "not a contract type" traps

A recurring source of error in reading United States defense contracting is the habit of treating any prominent acronym as though it named a *type of contract* or a *pricing arrangement*. Many of the field's most visible labels do neither. They describe a **program** or statutory **authority**, a **solicitation method**, a **customer or funding context**, a **competition rule**, an **acquisition classification**, or a **structural unit** of a contract — each of which answers a different one of the dimensions set out in [The dimensions of a federal contract](01-dimensions-of-a-federal-contract.md). This closing chapter collects the terms most often misfiled as contract or pricing types, maps each to the dimension it actually belongs to, and gives short notes on the ones that cause the most trouble. The governing principle, stated at the end, is the one this reference applies throughout: always say which dimension a term belongs to, and the confusion dissolves.

## The map: term, what it is, and where it belongs

None of the terms below is a value on the pricing-and-risk dimension developed in [Pricing types and cost risk](08-pricing-types-and-cost-risk.md), and most are not contract instruments at all. The table gives, for each, what it actually is and the dimension to which it belongs.

| Term | What it actually is | Dimension it belongs to |
|---|---|---|
| **SBIR / STTR** | Federal research and small-business innovation programs | Program / authority |
| **OTA** (Other Transaction authority) | A non-FAR contracting authority, generally outside the standard procurement-contract framework | Program / authority |
| **BAA** (Broad Agency Announcement) | A competitive solicitation method, used especially for research | Solicitation method |
| **CSO** (Commercial Solutions Opening) | A solicitation method for innovative commercial items | Solicitation method |
| **FMS** (Foreign Military Sales) | A customer and funding context (sales to foreign governments) | Customer / funding context |
| **BIC** (Best-in-Class) | A governmentwide designation applied to a vehicle | Vehicle designation |
| **Set-aside** | A restriction on who may compete | Competition |
| **Sole source** | A method of competing (or not competing) a requirement | Competition |
| **Commercial product / commercial service** | An acquisition classification under FAR Part 12 | Acquisition classification |
| **UCA / letter contract** | An undefinitized or preliminary contractual arrangement permitting work to start before final terms are negotiated | Pricing-definitization status |
| **CLIN** (Contract Line Item Number) | A unit of contract structure, funding, delivery, or pricing | Structural unit |
| **Modification** | A change to an existing contract or order | Contract action (not a new contract) |

Each row answers a different dimensional question — *which program or authority, which solicitation method, which customer, which competition rule, which classification, which definitization status, which structural unit, or which kind of action* — and none answers "how is the contractor paid." The remaining sections expand the entries that are most often mistaken for contract or pricing types.

### Programs and authorities: SBIR, STTR, and OTA

**SBIR** (Small Business Innovation Research) and **STTR** (Small Business Technology Transfer) are research and small-business innovation **programs**, not contract or pricing types. A given SBIR award is funded and competed under the program's rules, but the resulting instrument is still classified on the ordinary dimensions — it has an instrument form, a pricing type, a period of performance, and so on. Describing an award as "an SBIR" identifies the program that produced it, in much the same way "small business" identifies a competition-and-eligibility status rather than a contract type, as developed in [Small business in defense contracting](13-small-business.md).

**OTA** — **Other Transaction authority** — is the most consequential entry in this chapter, because it is the one most often misread as a kind of contract. OTA is a statutory **authority** to enter into transactions that are generally *outside* the standard FAR procurement-contract framework. An "Other Transaction" is therefore not a FAR contract type at all, and the pricing-type and ordering vocabulary built on the FAR — firm-fixed-price, IDIQ, fair opportunity — does not map onto it in the ordinary way. The correct statement is dimensional: OTA answers the question *under what authority is the Government acting*, and the answer "an Other Transaction" places the instrument outside, rather than within, the body of rules that the rest of this reference describes. Calling an Other Transaction a "contract type" both misstates its legal nature and invites the assumption that the standard FAR mechanics apply to it.

### Solicitation methods: BAA and CSO

A **Broad Agency Announcement (BAA)** is a competitive **solicitation method**, used especially to solicit research proposals and basic and applied science. A **Commercial Solutions Opening (CSO)** is likewise a **solicitation method**, oriented toward acquiring innovative commercial items. Neither term describes how the contractor is paid or what ordering structure the award uses. Both answer the question *how was this requirement solicited and competed* — a method dimension that is logically prior to, and independent of, the instrument and pricing that the resulting award carries. A BAA or CSO can lead to a fixed-price award, a cost-reimbursement award, or, in some cases, an Other Transaction; the solicitation method does not determine the pricing type any more than the choice to advertise a job determines the salary. Treating "BAA" or "CSO" as a contract type collapses the solicitation-method dimension into the instrument and pricing dimensions that it sits above.

### Customer and funding context: FMS

**Foreign Military Sales (FMS)** is a **customer and funding context**: the United States Government sells defense articles and services to a foreign government, and the foreign customer's funds (or authorized financing) pay for them. FMS is neither an instrument nor a pricing type. The underlying acquisition still uses ordinary contract instruments and ordinary pricing arrangements; what "FMS" tells the reader is *who the ultimate customer is and whose money funds the work*, which is a context distinct from how the contract is structured or priced. Reading "FMS" as a contract type obscures the fact that an FMS case is implemented through the same kinds of contracts as a domestic acquisition.

### Vehicle designation: BIC

**Best-in-Class (BIC)** is a governmentwide **designation** applied to a contract vehicle that has been recognized as a preferred solutionwide option. It is a label attached to a vehicle, not a new instrument or pricing type and not a property of any individual order. Like the additive reading of GWAC and Schedule developed in [IDV, IDIQ, MAC, GWAC, and Schedules](07-idv-idiq-mac-gwac-schedules.md), the correct reading of BIC is layered: a designated vehicle is still whatever instrument it already was — typically a multiple-award IDIQ or a Schedule — with a governmentwide designation added on top. "BIC" describes the vehicle's standing, not its mechanics.

### Competition rules: set-aside and sole source

A **set-aside** is a value on the **competition** dimension: it restricts *who may compete* for a requirement, most commonly reserving it for small businesses or a socioeconomic category. A **sole-source** award is also a competition-dimension concept: it describes a requirement that is awarded without competition, to a single source, generally on a documented justification such as only one responsible source under FAR 6.302-1.[^c18-far63021] Neither term names an instrument or a pricing type. Crucially, the competition dimension is independent of the award-structure dimension — the number of vehicle holders — so that *single-award* (one holder) is not the same thing as *sole source* (no competition), and a single-award contract can be the product of vigorous competition. That distinction is the subject of [Single-award, single-source, and sole-source](16-single-award-and-sole-source.md). Set-asides as an eligibility mechanism, and the rule of two that drives them, are developed in [Small business in defense contracting](13-small-business.md). Reading "set-aside" or "sole source" as a contract type fuses the competition question with the instrument and pricing questions that answer entirely different things.

### Acquisition classification: commercial product or service

A **commercial product** or **commercial service** is an **acquisition classification** under FAR Part 12, not a pricing type. The two are defined terms in FAR 2.101, where they describe what is being bought rather than how it is priced.[^c18-far2101] The classification governs which streamlined procedures and clauses apply and reflects a determination that the item or service is of a type sold or offered in the commercial marketplace. A commercial-item acquisition still carries an ordinary pricing type — most often firm-fixed-price, but not necessarily — and an ordinary instrument. "Commercial" answers the question *how is this item classified for purposes of applicable procedures*, which is distinct from how the contractor is paid. Treating the commercial classification as though it were itself the pricing type conflates the FAR Part 12 classification dimension with the pricing-and-risk dimension treated in [Pricing types and cost risk](08-pricing-types-and-cost-risk.md).

### Definitization status: UCA and letter contract

An **undefinitized contract action (UCA)**, the broad form of which is the **letter contract**, is a preliminary or **undefinitized** contractual arrangement that authorizes the contractor to begin work before the final terms — most importantly the price or estimated cost — have been negotiated. It is not a distinct pricing type; it is a *status* on the way to a definitized pricing type. The arrangement exists precisely because the parties have agreed to start work before agreeing on price, and it is later **definitized** into firm terms through a modification, a process noted among the kinds of modification in [Contract modifications](14-contract-modifications.md). The correct dimensional statement is that a UCA or letter contract describes *whether the price has yet been settled*, not *how the contractor will ultimately be paid*. Reading a UCA as a pricing type misses that its defining feature is the absence, for the moment, of a settled price.

### Structural unit and contract action: CLIN and modification

A **Contract Line Item Number (CLIN)** is a **structural unit** of a contract — a numbered line that organizes what is being bought, how it is funded, when it is delivered, and how it is priced. A single contract or order routinely carries several CLINs, and different CLINs on the same order may even carry different pricing types, as the worked example in [The dimensions of a federal contract](01-dimensions-of-a-federal-contract.md) shows. A CLIN is therefore a unit *within* a contract, not a contract or a pricing type itself; the CLIN structure, its sub-line items, and funding traceability are the subject of [CLINs, SLINs, and funding traceability](15-clins-slins-and-funding-traceability.md).

A **modification** is a **contract action** — a written change to an existing contract or order — and is not inherently a new contract. It is defined by its relationship to an already-awarded instrument rather than by its size, so a modification can be administrative or substantive, funded or unfunded, tiny or among the largest actions in a program, as [Contract modifications](14-contract-modifications.md) develops. The recurring error is to read a large modification as if it were a new award, or to read the word "modification" as conveying something about pricing; it conveys only that an existing instrument was changed.

## Making the innovation pathways operational

The four innovation labels in the map above — SBIR/STTR, Other Transactions, the Broad Agency Announcement, and the Commercial Solutions Opening — are the entries most likely to be mistaken not only for contract types but for interchangeable on-ramps. They are not interchangeable. Each is a distinct route with its own way for a firm to enter, its own range of resulting instruments, its own mechanism for moving from an early effort to follow-on work, its own answer to whether that follow-on must be competed again, and its own footprint in public award data. The map is enough to file each label under the right dimension; the four facets below describe how each pathway actually operates, which is what determines where a prospective offeror enters and how the resulting action appears — or fails to appear — to an outside analyst.

The pathways share a common shape: an early, lower-dollar effort (a study, a prototype, or a research project) followed, if it succeeds, by a transition decision and then by some larger follow-on. What differs is the legal machinery at each step. The table states the five facets compactly; the paragraphs that follow expand each pathway and identify the statutory authority that makes its follow-on lawful.

| Pathway | How a firm enters | Resulting instrument | Follow-on mechanism | Re-competed? | Public-data footprint |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| **SBIR / STTR** | Competitive topic proposal, by phase (I → II) | FAR contract, grant, or other instrument | Phase III deriving from the SBIR/STTR work | No — Phase III may be sole-source and need not be re-competed | Phase I/II visible; Phase III award may appear as an ordinary (often sole-source) action |
| **Other Transaction (OT)** | Often via a consortium or direct solicitation; research or prototype project | Other Transaction agreement (non-FAR) | Production OT following a successful prototype OT | No — production follow-on without further competition when the original solicitation contemplated it | Variable and uneven OT reporting; the production follow-on need not surface as an open competition |
| **BAA** | White paper or proposal against a standing announcement | FAR contract (or assistance instrument) | A later FAR action; a BAA is a method, not a follow-on engine | A new requirement is solicited and reviewed on its own | Solicitation and resulting contracts generally visible as ordinary actions |
| **CSO** | Proposal against a Commercial Solutions Opening for an innovative commercial item | FAR contract **or** OT | Depends on the instrument chosen (a FAR follow-on or an OT production follow-on) | Depends on the instrument and on what the CSO contemplated | Visible as a solicitation; the resulting instrument's footprint follows that instrument |

### Other Transactions (OT): research and prototype authorities, and the production follow-on

An Other Transaction is not a FAR contract, as the map records; operationally, the distinction that matters most is between the two statutory authorities and what each enables downstream. A **research OT** is entered under 10 U.S.C. 4021 for basic, applied, and advanced research projects, while a **prototype OT** is entered under 10 U.S.C. 4022 for prototype projects directly relevant to enhancing the mission effectiveness of military personnel and supporting systems.[^c18-ot] A firm frequently enters either through a **consortium** that manages a portfolio of projects for a sponsoring agency, or through a direct solicitation; the resulting instrument is an OT agreement negotiated outside the standard FAR procurement-contract framework. The operationally decisive feature is the follow-on: a **competitively awarded prototype OT** that is **completed successfully** can support a **follow-on production** action — itself an OT — **without further competition**, provided the original solicitation and resulting agreement expressly **contemplated** the production follow-on. That authority, implemented for DoD at DFARS 206.001-70, is what allows a prototype effort to mature into production without a fresh open competition, and it is why "competitively awarded" and "completed successfully" are not incidental adjectives but the statutory preconditions for the production transition.[^c18-ot]

### SBIR / STTR: the three-phase structure and the Phase III follow-on

The SBIR and STTR programs, authorized under 15 U.S.C. 638, are operationally a **three-phase** progression rather than a single award. A firm enters by winning a competitive proposal against a published topic: **Phase I** establishes technical feasibility; **Phase II** develops the technology, typically to a prototype; and **Phase III** is the commercialization or transition stage in which the technology derived from the Phase I/II work is brought into use, including by another agency or program.[^c18-sbir] The transition mechanism is the defining operational fact: a **Phase III award derives from** the earlier SBIR/STTR work, **may be awarded sole-source**, and **need not be re-competed**, because the competition that justifies it occurred at the Phase I/II stage. A Phase III action is not confined to a single instrument form — it can be a **FAR contract or another instrument** — so the same authority can surface as an ordinary contract action even though no new competition preceded it. For an outside analyst, a Phase III award entered by a prior SBIR awardee is the pathway's least visible step precisely because it can look like any other sole-source action.

### Broad Agency Announcement (BAA): a research solicitation method

A Broad Agency Announcement is a **solicitation method** under FAR 35.016, used for **basic and applied research** and that part of advanced technology development not directed at a specific system or hardware solution.[^c18-baa] A firm enters by submitting a white paper or proposal against a standing announcement that may remain open for an extended period. The operational character that distinguishes a BAA from ordinary source selection is its **evaluation**: proposals undergo **peer or scientific review** against the announcement's stated criteria, rather than head-to-head price competition among offerors for a single defined requirement. A BAA results in **FAR contracts** (or, where appropriate, assistance instruments), and it is best understood as a method for soliciting research, not as a follow-on engine: any later production or scale-up is a separate action taken under whatever authority then applies, not an automatic continuation of the BAA award.

### Commercial Solutions Opening (CSO): a competitive method for innovative commercial items

A Commercial Solutions Opening is a **competitive solicitation method** oriented toward acquiring **innovative commercial items, technologies, and services**.[^c18-cso] A firm enters by responding to the CSO with a proposed solution, which is evaluated on a competitive basis under the opening's criteria. The operationally important point is that a CSO is **instrument-agnostic at the output**: it can result in a **FAR contract or an Other Transaction**, and the follow-on consequences track whichever instrument is chosen — a FAR award carries ordinary FAR follow-on mechanics, while an OT award can carry the prototype-to-production follow-on described above when the CSO and resulting agreement contemplated it. Because the CSO is a method rather than an instrument, reading it as a contract type obscures the more useful question of which instrument it produced and therefore which follow-on rules attach.

### The innovation pathway, end to end

The four pathways converge on a common sequence: an entry method produces an early effort, a transition decision follows, and — if funded — the effort matures into a larger follow-on under one of several instruments. The diagram traces that sequence and marks the point at which efforts most often stall.

```text
Innovation pathway: entry → early effort → transition → follow-on

  ENTRY METHOD / AUTHORITY
  ┌───────────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │  BAA   │   CSO   │   SBIR/STTR   │  prototype  │
  │ (35.016)│        │  (15 U.S.C.   │     OT      │
  │        │        │     638)      │ (10 U.S.C.  │
  │        │        │               │    4022)    │
  └───────────────────────┬───────────────────────┘
                          │
                          ▼
  EARLY EFFORT
  ┌───────────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │  prototype  ·  study  ·  demonstration  ·  R&D │
  └───────────────────────┬───────────────────────┘
                          │

  TRANSITION DECISION  +  TRANSITION FUNDING   ◄── many efforts stall here

  │  Is the effort funded into production/scale?   │     see funding traceability)
```

The hinge in the middle — the **transition decision and the transition funding behind it** — is where many otherwise successful efforts stall, because a completed prototype or a finished Phase II does not by itself carry an appropriation into production. Whether transition money exists, in what color, and on which line item is the question developed in [CLINs, SLINs, and funding traceability](15-clins-slins-and-funding-traceability.md); a technically successful effort with no funded follow-on line item simply stops at the hinge.

### How these routes relate to competition posture and to recompete intelligence

These statutory routes are properly read as a competition-dimension fact, but a distinct one. A **Phase III** award and a **production OT** following a successful prototype both proceed **lawfully without ordinary open competition** — yet they are *not* instances of the general sole-source posture that [Single-award, single-source, and sole-source](16-single-award-and-sole-source.md) develops. That chapter owns the FAR Part 6 sole-source concept, in which an award departs from full and open competition on a documented justification; the innovation follow-ons here instead rest on their own statutory authorities (15 U.S.C. 638 for Phase III; 10 U.S.C. 4022 with DFARS 206.001-70 for the production OT), under which the absence of a fresh competition is built into the authority rather than justified as an exception to it. The two should be cross-read, not merged: both yield an action with no head-to-head competition, but on different legal bases.

The same feature creates a specific data trap. Because a Phase III award or a production-OT follow-on lawfully proceeds **without a new competition, it will not appear as an open competition** in opportunity data — there is no fresh solicitation to observe. An analyst scanning for competed opportunities will therefore systematically miss work that flows through these routes, which is the **innovation-follow-on trap** treated in [Recompetes and opportunity intelligence](05-recompetes-and-opportunity-intelligence.md): the follow-on materializes as a sole-source-looking action (or, for an OT, as uneven and sometimes sparse reporting) rather than as a contestable opening. Finally, these pathways differ from ordinary FAR contracting in their **data-rights** terms — SBIR/STTR data rights and OT intellectual-property provisions are negotiated on bases distinct from the standard FAR clauses — a difference developed in [Prime–subcontractor structure, privity, and flowdowns](12-prime-contracting-subcontracting-teaming.md).

## The governing principle

Every term in this chapter is frequently misread as a "type of contract" or a pricing arrangement when in fact each answers a different dimensional question. SBIR, STTR, and OTA answer *which program or authority*; BAA and CSO answer *which solicitation method*; FMS answers *which customer and funding context*; BIC answers *which designation a vehicle carries*; set-aside and sole source answer *which competition rule*; commercial product or service answers *which acquisition classification*; a UCA or letter contract answers *whether the price has been definitized*; a CLIN answers *which structural unit*; and a modification answers *what kind of action* was taken. The operating rule of this entire reference, set out first in [The core vocabulary](02-core-vocabulary.md), is that a term should always be paired with the dimension it belongs to. A term correctly assigned to its dimension is rarely misread; a term borrowed across dimensions — pricing read onto a program, a solicitation method read as an instrument, a context read as a contract — almost always is. Map each label to its dimension, and the apparent tangle of acronyms resolves into a set of independent, individually answerable questions about the same award.

The one dimension genuinely concerned with pricing arrangements — the choice among fixed-price, incentive, cost-reimbursement, and time-and-materials contract types — is the subject of FAR Part 16. As of the article date (June 25, 2026), DoD contracting officers apply the Revolutionary FAR Overhaul rewrite of FAR Part 16 under a class deviation effective March 16, 2026.[^c18-overhaul] That deviation governs how pricing arrangements are selected; it does not turn any of the programs, authorities, solicitation methods, customer contexts, designations, competition rules, classifications, or structural units cataloged in this chapter into a pricing type.[^c18-deviation]
