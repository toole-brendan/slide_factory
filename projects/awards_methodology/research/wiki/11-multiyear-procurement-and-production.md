# Multiyear procurement and major weapon production

**Multiyear procurement (MYP)** is a funding-and-commitment method by which the Government buys requirements for more than one program year under a single contract without exercising a separate option for each future year. It belongs to the funding dimension of an award — the axis that asks *when and how money is obligated and committed* — and not to the instrument, pricing, or competition dimensions described in [The dimensions of a federal contract](01-dimensions-of-a-federal-contract.md). Because major weapons such as aircraft, missiles, ships, and submarines are frequently bought in defined production lots rather than in uncertain quantities, the production contracts that carry MYP are also a leading example of awards that are *not* indefinite-delivery vehicles at all, which makes this dimension the natural place to separate the funding method from the instrument and from the structure of the supplier base.

## What multiyear procurement is

A **multiyear contract** purchases requirements for more than one — but generally not more than five — program years without requiring the Government to exercise a separate option for every future program year. The defining feature is the up-front commitment: more than one year's requirement is contractually included at the initial award, rather than held in reserve as options to be decided annually. Performance in later years may still be contingent on appropriations being enacted, and to protect the contractor against an up-front commitment that the budget later does not fund, the contract may provide a **cancellation payment** — and an associated cancellation ceiling — if those appropriations are not made. These features are governed by FAR Subpart 17.1.[^c11-far171] The "generally not more than five program years" span is a structural limit of the method and is recorded as such throughout this reference.

For DoD, multiyear procurement carries additional machinery beyond the FAR baseline. A defense MYP usually involves a present-value cost comparison demonstrating savings against annual contracting, particular budget treatment, statutory findings, congressional authorization or notification where applicable, and explicit controls over cancellation liability and advance procurement. These DoD-specific requirements are set out in DFARS Subpart 217.1, which implements the statutory multiyear and advance-procurement provisions of 10 U.S.C.[^c11-dfars]

## Multiyear procurement versus a base contract with options

The cleanest way to see what MYP is, is to contrast it with the more common structure of an annual base contract carrying [options](09-options-and-award-terms.md). Both span several years, but they commit the Government very differently. Under an options structure the future-year work is *not yet committed*: the Government may decline to exercise an option, and the contractor usually has no recovery for the work in an option that is never exercised. Under a multiyear contract, more than one year's requirement is part of the basic deal from the outset, no annual option exercise is needed for the basic multiyear quantities, and the contractor's exposure to an up-front commitment is offset by cancellation protection.

| Issue | Annual contract with options | Multiyear contract |
|---|---|---|
| Future-year work at initial award | Not yet committed; the Government may decline options | More than one year's requirement is contractually included |
| Annual decision | The Government must exercise each option | No annual option exercise is needed for the basic multiyear quantities |
| Appropriations risk | The Government can decline an option | Later performance may be contingent on appropriations |
| Contractor protection | Usually no recovery for an unexercised option | May include cancellation charges or a cancellation ceiling |
| Typical use | Recurring services and ordinary acquisitions | Stable, known production or service requirements where multiyear commitment creates value |
| Production economics | Limited commitment to future lots | May support economic order quantities, stable production rates, long-lead purchases, and amortization of nonrecurring costs |

The last row is the economic reason the method exists. Because a multiyear commitment gives a contractor and its suppliers confidence that several lots will actually be built, it can support buying materials in economical quantities, holding production rates stable, purchasing long-lead items in advance, and amortizing nonrecurring costs across the committed quantity — efficiencies that a year-by-year options buy, with its limited commitment to future lots, cannot reliably unlock.

## Related but distinct concepts

Several concepts travel alongside multiyear procurement and are frequently conflated with it, but each is a separate idea:

- **Economic order quantity (EOQ)** is the practice of purchasing components or materials in economical quantities across future production lots. It is a buying efficiency that a multiyear commitment can enable, not the commitment itself.
- **Advance procurement** is the purchase of long-lead components before the main end-item procurement year, so that material is on hand when assembly begins. DoD controls over advance procurement are part of the DFARS multiyear machinery, but advance procurement can also occur outside a formal MYP.
- **Block buy contracting** is another multiyear quantity-commitment approach with its own statutory treatment, distinct from MYP under FAR Subpart 17.1.
- **Incremental funding** is the practice of adding funds to a contract or order over time. Incremental funding alone does not make a contract a multiyear procurement; the two answer different questions, as [The dimensions of a federal contract](01-dimensions-of-a-federal-contract.md) notes, and incremental funding is treated in the funding-traceability material at [CLINs, SLINs, and funding traceability](15-clins-slins-and-funding-traceability.md).
- A **multiple-year contract** is an ordinary descriptive phrase, often used for a base period plus options that happens to run across several years. It is not the same as the defined multiyear *method*, and the resemblance of the words is a frequent source of error of the kind catalogued in [Common "not a contract type" traps](18-not-a-contract-type-traps.md).

### Multiyear procurement is not a pricing type

The single most important classification point is that **multiyear procurement is principally a commitment, budgeting, and production-economics method, not a pricing type**. A defense MYP very commonly uses a fixed-price arrangement — frequently fixed-price incentive — but the multiyear commitment and the pricing arrangement are separate classifications on separate axes. The pricing dimension, developed in [Pricing types and cost risk](08-pricing-types-and-cost-risk.md), asks how the contractor is paid and who bears cost risk; the multiyear method asks how many years of requirement are committed and how that commitment is budgeted and protected. A contract can be multiyear and firm-fixed-price, multiyear and fixed-price-incentive, or in principle multiyear with other pricing, just as the instrument and pricing dimensions are independent of each other.

## Why major weapon production is often not an IDIQ

Reporting on weapon programs sometimes assumes that any large, recurring defense buy must be an indefinite-delivery indefinite-quantity (IDIQ) vehicle. For major production, that assumption is usually wrong. Defined production lots of aircraft, missiles, ships, and submarines are frequently purchased through standalone definitive contracts, multiyear procurement contracts, production options, lot contracts, and contract modifications that add authorized production quantities — none of which is an indefinite-delivery vehicle.

The reason is structural. An IDIQ exists to handle requirements whose quantity is genuinely uncertain at award, which is why FAR requires a stated minimum and maximum for that form; the indefinite-delivery framework is the subject of [Indefinite-delivery contracts](06-indefinite-delivery-contracts.md). For a production lot, the Government usually knows the number of end items it is funding — a specific number of airframes, missiles, or ships — so an indefinite-quantity arrangement is less natural than it is for the uncertain demand of engineering support. Buying *specified* quantities is exactly what a definitive contract, a multiyear contract, or a quantity-adding modification does well. The corollary is that the *supporting* work for the same weapon system — engineering services, repair, spare parts, or technical assistance, where future demand is genuinely uncertain — may still be acquired through IDIQs or basic ordering agreements (BOAs). Two awards on the same program can therefore sit on different points of the instrument axis: a definitive production contract for the end items and an IDIQ for the sustainment around them.

## The Virginia-class submarine example

The construction of *Virginia*-class nuclear-powered attack submarines is a worked case in which all the dimensions diverge, and it is frequently mis-described. The shorthand that *Virginia*-class boats are bought through "a sole-source IDIQ that always goes to Electric Boat" collapses at least four separate dimensions into one phrase and is inaccurate on each. The prime is **General Dynamics Electric Boat (GDEB)** — a General Dynamics shipyard, not General Electric — and the instrument is not an IDIQ.

### The industrial base and the teaming structure

Only two U.S. shipbuilders currently construct nuclear-powered submarines: General Dynamics Electric Boat and HII Newport News Shipbuilding. That extraordinarily limited industrial base makes ordinary marketplace competition unrealistic for complete nuclear-submarine construction.[^c11-gao] Within that base, the *Virginia* program runs on a long-standing teaming arrangement: GDEB is the prime contractor under the current teaming structure, Newport News Shipbuilding is the major subcontractor and co-builder, both shipyards construct major submarine sections, and both have delivered completed *Virginia*-class submarines. The Navy describes the arrangement as a teaming arrangement between GDEB and Newport News, not as Electric Boat performing the entire program alone — a prime-and-subcontractor relationship of the kind analyzed in [Prime–subcontractor structure, privity, and flowdowns](12-prime-contracting-subcontracting-teaming.md).

### The 2019 Block V action, by dimension

The December 2, 2019 Block V action is best read by separating its dimensions rather than forcing it into a single label:

```text
Virginia-class Block V (announced Dec. 2, 2019)
│
├── Instrument ............ existing definitive production contract
├── Action .............. $22.2 billion contract modification
├── Pricing type ........ fixed-price incentive
├── Funding / commitment . multiyear procurement
├── Quantity ............ nine submarines
├── Option .............. one additional submarine
├── Prime ............... General Dynamics Electric Boat
└── Major subcontractor . HII Newport News Shipbuilding
```

The action was a $22.2 billion modification to an existing definitive production contract, priced fixed-price-incentive, structured as a multiyear procurement, for nine submarines plus an option for one more.[^c11-blockv] It was not an IDIQ: the Navy was buying specified submarines in a defined production block, not reserving an uncertain quantity. A modification of this magnitude is unremarkable on its own axis — the point that a modification can be enormous, and need not be a new contract, is developed in [Contract modifications](14-contract-modifications.md).

### The 2025 addition

In April 2025 the Navy again used contract modifications, this time to add two FY2024 *Virginia*-class submarines together with related shipyard investments. The announced actions involved both GDEB and Newport News contracts, with more than $9.4 billion obligated at award from Shipbuilding and Conversion, Navy funds.[^c11-2025] As in 2019, the vehicle was a modification adding specified production quantities, not an indefinite-delivery order.

### Why Electric Boat is the prime — and the right competition baseline

That GDEB holds the prime role is a matter of industrial-base reality rather than the absence of a competition that could otherwise have occurred. The relevant factors include GDEB's role as design authority and lead yard; its nuclear-submarine-specific facilities and workforce; its existing design, production, testing, and integration systems; the practical impossibility of quickly creating another qualified nuclear-submarine prime; the Navy's deliberate allocation of work between the two existing nuclear-capable shipyards; and the need to maintain the submarine industrial base. The economically meaningful competition for complete nuclear-submarine construction is therefore not comparable to a normal information-technology services solicitation with twenty capable bidders. The common FAR Part 6 basis for limited competition captures this: only one responsible source — or, for DoD, one or a limited number of responsible sources — can satisfy the requirement.[^c11-far6302] This is a competition-dimension judgment, and it is distinct from the *single-award* and *sole-source* labels analyzed in [Single-award, single-source, and sole-source](16-single-award-and-sole-source.md); a limited industrial base is not the same thing as a one-holder vehicle.

### A more accurate description

Read across its dimensions, the program is described accurately as follows: *Virginia*-class submarines are acquired through large definitive, multiyear production contracts and modifications, with GDEB as prime and Newport News as a major subcontractor and co-builder within a two-shipyard nuclear-submarine industrial base. Laid out by axis, the same action separates cleanly — instrument: definitive production contract; action: contract modification; commitment method: multiyear procurement; pricing type: fixed-price incentive; competition: limited industrial-base environment; prime structure: GDEB prime, HII–Newport News major subcontractor; deliverables: specified submarines. The example shows why the dimensions should be tracked separately: every one of these answers a different question, and no single phrase such as "sole-source IDIQ" can carry all of them at once.
