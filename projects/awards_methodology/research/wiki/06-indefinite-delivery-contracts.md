# Indefinite-delivery contracts (FAR 16.5)

FAR Subpart 16.5 is not a single contract type. It is the regulatory framework for **indefinite-delivery contracts** and the orders placed under them, and it answers two of the dimensions developed in [The dimensions of a federal contract](01-dimensions-of-a-federal-contract.md): the *instrument* dimension (what legal vehicle is used when the quantity or timing of a requirement is not known at award) and the *ordering* dimension (whether work is bought at the parent level or through later task or delivery orders). The most common error in reading this part of the vocabulary is to treat "indefinite-delivery" as the name of a contract — it is instead an umbrella over three distinct forms, a body of rules for how holders compete for orders, and a vocabulary of overlapping time periods. This chapter sets out the three forms, the order types, the single- versus multiple-award distinction, the fair-opportunity process and its dollar thresholds, the exceptions to fair opportunity, the several ordering clocks, and the restricted regime for protesting orders.

## The three indefinite-delivery forms

FAR Subpart 16.5 recognizes three indefinite-delivery forms, distinguished by how much is fixed at award and how much is deferred to later orders.

- A **definite-quantity contract** fixes the total quantity at award, but the timing or location of delivery may be determined through later orders. For example, the Government commits to buy exactly 1,000 generators, with delivery locations designated as needs arise. What is "indefinite" is the delivery schedule, not the amount.
- A **requirements contract** is the Government's agreement that designated activities will purchase all of their actual covered requirements from one contractor during the ordering period. The precise quantity is unknown, and the stated estimate is **not a guaranteed quantity**. For example, one installation agrees to purchase all of its covered copier-maintenance requirements from one contractor for three years; if its actual need turns out to be larger or smaller than the estimate, the contractor must meet the actual need and is not owed the estimate.
- An **indefinite-quantity contract**, universally abbreviated **IDIQ**, is used when the Government knows the general category of supplies or services and commits to a stated minimum but does not know the exact quantity or timing. The contractor must perform orders up to a stated maximum. For example, between $10,000 and $500 million of engineering services over five years. FAR 16.504 requires that the contract state a minimum greater than a nominal quantity and a stated maximum, so that the minimum represents a real purchase commitment and the maximum represents the contractual ceiling.[^c06-far16504] The indefinite-quantity clause that establishes this minimum-and-maximum obligation is FAR 52.216-22.[^c06-clause]

The IDIQ is by far the most common of the three in defense contracting, and the rest of this chapter is written principally about it; the requirements and definite-quantity forms borrow the same ordering vocabulary. The relationship of the IDIQ to the broader family of indefinite-delivery vehicles — and to the federal data category that holds them — is treated separately in [IDV, IDIQ, MAC, GWAC, and Schedules](07-idv-idiq-mac-gwac-schedules.md).

## Task orders versus delivery orders

Within the indefinite-delivery framework, work is acquired through orders, and the order type follows what the order buys. A **task-order contract** acquires services through orders called **task orders**; a **delivery-order contract** acquires supplies through orders called **delivery orders**. A single order is itself a binding contract within the parent framework, even though it is not a separate prime contract with the Government. Mixed vehicles can contain both supplies and services, but the terminology usually follows what an individual order acquires, so the same parent vehicle may issue task orders for the service portions of a requirement and delivery orders for the supply portions. The distinction is one of nomenclature and the kind of work being bought; it is independent of the pricing type carried on the order's contract line items, which is the subject of [Pricing types and cost risk](08-pricing-types-and-cost-risk.md).

## Single-award versus multiple-award IDIQ

The terms **single-award** and **multiple-award** describe how many primes hold the vehicle, not how the holders are paid or whether they competed. A **single-award IDIQ** gives one prime the vehicle, and later orders are placed with that one contractor. A **multiple-award IDIQ** gives several primes parallel contracts for the same scope; those holders ordinarily compete against one another for later orders. The holders of a multiple-award vehicle do not jointly hold one contract, and none of them owns the entire ceiling — each holds a separate prime contract under the common solicitation.

The agency-specific terms **MAC IDIQ**, **MATOC** (multiple-award task-order contract), **MADOC** (multiple-award delivery-order contract), and **MACC** (multiple-award construction contract) are variations on this same multiple-award architecture, not new pricing types or new instruments. They name the combination of "multiple award" with a particular order type or mission area. Because the single-award versus multiple-award choice is an award-structure question and not a competition question, a single-award vehicle is not the same thing as a sole-source award and may itself be the product of vigorous competition — a distinction developed in [Single-award, single-source, and sole-source](16-single-award-and-sole-source.md).

## Fair opportunity and its thresholds

On a multiple-award vehicle, eligible holders ordinarily receive a **fair opportunity** to be considered for orders above the micro-purchase threshold. Fair opportunity is not the same as reopening full and open competition to the entire marketplace: the field is limited to the existing holders of the vehicle. The ordering contracting officer has broad discretion to streamline the process, and the order competition is generally **not** governed by the FAR Part 6 competition procedures, FAR Part 14 sealed-bidding procedures, or FAR Subpart 15.3 source-selection procedures that apply to awarding the parent vehicles themselves. Price or cost must still be considered in selecting the order awardee.

The intensity of the fair-opportunity process scales with the value of the order. As of the current thresholds, effective October 1, 2025, the **micro-purchase threshold** is $15,000 and the **simplified acquisition threshold** is $350,000.[^c06-thresholds] The tiers operate as follows:

| Order value | Fair-opportunity treatment |
|---|---|
| At or below the micro-purchase threshold ($15,000) | Fair opportunity does not apply; the order may be placed directly. |
| Above the micro-purchase threshold through the simplified acquisition threshold ($350,000) | The contracting officer may fairly consider holders using available information and need not necessarily request a new response from every holder. |
| Above the simplified acquisition threshold through $7.5 million | Fair notice of the intent to make a purchase is ordinarily given to all holders capable of performing the work. |
| Above $7.5 million | The notice, evaluation factors, documentation, and post-award process become more formal, including a more developed evaluation and a debriefing on request. |

These dollar figures change on their own cycle and should be read as dated facts rather than as permanent features of the framework; the conceptual structure of escalating formality is what endures.

## Exceptions to fair opportunity

The duty to provide fair opportunity is subject to a closed set of exceptions. A holder need not be given fair opportunity when:

- the agency's need for the supplies or services is so urgent that providing fair opportunity would result in unacceptable delay;
- only one awardee is capable of providing supplies or services that are unique or highly specialized;
- the order must be issued on a sole-source basis as a logical follow-on to an order already issued under the contract that was placed competitively;
- it is necessary to place an order to satisfy a minimum guarantee owed to a holder; or
- a statute expressly requires or authorizes that the purchase be made from a specified source.

These determinations are documented through a **justification for an exception to fair opportunity (JEFO)**. A JEFO is related to, but not identical with, the FAR Part 6 **justification and approval (J&A)** for other than full and open competition: the J&A justifies departing from full and open competition for an award, whereas the JEFO justifies departing from fair opportunity among existing holders for an order. Conflating the two documents is a common reading error; the broader vocabulary of single-source and sole-source justification is developed in [Single-award, single-source, and sole-source](16-single-award-and-sole-source.md).

## Ordering period versus order period of performance

Indefinite-delivery contracts carry more than one clock, and the most common time-related error is to treat the life of the parent vehicle and the life of a particular order as the same thing. Four concepts must be kept distinct:

- The **ordering period** is the window during which the Government may issue new orders under the parent vehicle.
- The **order period of performance** is the window during which a particular order's work may be performed.
- A **parent option** extends the period during which new orders can be issued — it lengthens the ordering period.
- An **order option** extends or adds work under a particular child order — it lengthens or expands that one order's period of performance.

An order's performance may continue beyond the end of the parent ordering period when the parent terms so authorize; that does not ordinarily permit the Government to issue entirely new orders after the ordering period has ended. The practical consequences of this separation — including the question of what may and may not happen once an ordering period closes — are developed at length in [Period of performance and scope](10-period-of-performance-and-scope.md), and the distinction between parent-level and order-level options is developed in [Options and award terms](09-options-and-award-terms.md).

## Order protests

Protests against the placement of orders under indefinite-delivery contracts are restricted under the current revised FAR Part 16. An **order protest** generally may be brought only on the ground that an order increases the scope, period, or maximum value of the parent contract, or — for orders placed by the Department of Defense, the National Aeronautics and Space Administration, or the Coast Guard — on any ground when the order is valued above $35 million.[^c06-protests] Qualifying value-based order protests are heard by the Government Accountability Office (GAO). The general effect is that routine, in-scope orders below the agency-specific value threshold are largely insulated from protest, while the disputes that remain reviewable are those alleging that an order has burst the contractual boundaries of its parent or has crossed the high-value line.

## Citation note: codified FAR 16.505 versus DoD-deviation FAR 16.507

The location of these ordering procedures within FAR depends on which text governs. Under the Department of Defense class deviation adopting the Revolutionary FAR Overhaul, the fair-opportunity ordering procedures described in this chapter are located primarily at **FAR 16.507**.[^c06-deviation] The still-codified FAR places many of those same procedures at **FAR 16.505**. As of the article date, June 25, 2026, DoD contracting officers apply the deviation text, so a reader comparing a DoD order file against the codified FAR will find the fair-opportunity rules cited to 16.507 in the file and to 16.505 in the codified regulation. This reference gives the current DoD-deviation location and notes the codified one wherever the distinction matters.

[^c06-clause]: FAR 52.216-22, Indefinite Quantity, the clause establishing the contractor's obligation to furnish at least the stated minimum and up to the stated maximum during the ordering period. <https://www.acquisition.gov/far/52.216-22>.
