# IDV, IDIQ, MAC, GWAC, and Schedules

This chapter disambiguates a cluster of acronyms that are routinely collapsed into one another even though they live on different dimensions of an award. The unifying point is that **IDV** is a data classification, while **IDIQ**, **GWAC**, **Schedule**, **BPA**, and **BOA** name instruments or arrangements with different governing rules. The single most common error in reading federal contract data is to fuse the data label and the instrument into a single concept — to read "IDV" as though it were a contract type, or to assume that every umbrella vehicle is an IDIQ. The ordering mechanics that operate inside these vehicles — task versus delivery orders, fair opportunity and its exceptions, and the three indefinite-delivery forms (definite-quantity, requirements, and indefinite-quantity) — belong to [Indefinite-delivery contracts](06-indefinite-delivery-contracts.md) and are not repeated here; this chapter is about telling the vehicles and the data categories apart.

## IDV versus IDIQ

An **Indefinite Delivery Vehicle (IDV)** is the broad reporting and data category. It is the umbrella, used in federal contract reporting under FAR Subpart 4.6, for parent instruments against which orders or purchases may later be placed. An **indefinite-delivery indefinite-quantity (IDIQ) contract** is something narrower: one particular legally binding indefinite-quantity contract, containing a binding minimum and a stated maximum, with work bought through orders rather than at award. The relationship between the two is one of genus and species. Every reported IDIQ is an IDV, but not every IDV is an IDIQ.[^c07-idv]

The reason the broader category is not exhausted by IDIQ is that an IDV may also include Federal Supply Schedules, blanket purchase agreements (BPAs), basic ordering agreements (BOAs), and other agreements that contain ordering provisions — only some of which are indefinite-quantity contracts. Treating "IDV" and "IDIQ" as synonyms therefore makes two distinct mistakes at once: it mistakes a data classification for an instrument, and it silently excludes the Schedules, BPAs, and BOAs that the IDV category is specifically built to encompass. The same parent-versus-child logic that the data systems use to keep an IDV's ceiling from being added to its orders' values is developed in [How awards appear in federal data](04-contract-data-systems.md), and the underlying glossary entries are in [The core vocabulary](02-core-vocabulary.md).

## Multiple-award contract versus multi-agency contract

The acronym **MAC** carries two official and unrelated meanings, and conflating them is a frequent source of error because the two senses answer entirely different questions.

A **multiple-award contract** answers a question on the award-structure dimension: it is an arrangement in which more than one source receives an award for the same or similar scope. The defining facts are that each award is a separate prime contract and that no single holder owns the entire ceiling. After the parallel awards are made, specific requirements are competed among the holders through the fair-opportunity process described in [Indefinite-delivery contracts](06-indefinite-delivery-contracts.md). The characteristic misunderstanding is to imagine the awardees jointly holding one instrument, or to attribute the full vehicle ceiling to each company; both errors are addressed, on the money side, in [Awards, ceilings, obligations, and spending](03-awards-ceilings-obligations-spending.md).

A **multi-agency contract** answers a different question — who may order — and refers to a task- or delivery-order contract established by one agency that is usable by other agencies. Here the distinguishing feature is the breadth of authorized customers, not the number of holders. A single agency sets up the vehicle, and other agencies place orders against it under the vehicle's terms. The two senses are independent rather than mutually exclusive, so a single vehicle can be **both multiple-award and multi-agency**: established by one agency for governmentwide or interagency use, and held by several competing sources at once. Because the two meanings share three letters but sit on different dimensions, an analyst who does not specify which "MAC" is meant invites exactly the kind of cross-dimensional confusion this reference is built to prevent.

## GWAC

A **Governmentwide Acquisition Contract (GWAC)** is a task- or delivery-order contract for information technology, established by one agency for governmentwide use under designated authority. Its defining attributes are therefore its subject matter (information technology) and its access (governmentwide), conferred by a specific delegation of authority to the establishing agency.

In practice, GWACs are commonly structured as multiple-award IDIQs, which is precisely why the term is easy to misfile. The label "GWAC" does not name a new pricing arrangement or a new ordering mechanic; the ordering still proceeds through task and delivery orders under the rules covered in [Indefinite-delivery contracts](06-indefinite-delivery-contracts.md), and the pricing of individual orders still belongs to the separate pricing dimension developed in [Pricing types and cost risk](08-pricing-types-and-cost-risk.md). "GWAC" describes the vehicle's IT purpose and its governmentwide accessibility, layered on top of an instrument that is usually an IDIQ. The correct reading is additive: a GWAC is typically an IDIQ that has also been designated for IT and opened to the whole government, not a fourth species of contract that displaces the IDIQ category.

## GSA Multiple Award Schedule

The **GSA Multiple Award Schedule (MAS)** — the GSA Schedule — consists of many individual schedule contracts for commercial products and services held by a large population of vendors. Agencies place orders against those schedule contracts under FAR Subpart 8.4 and the associated GSA rules, rather than under the ordinary indefinite-delivery procedures of FAR Subpart 16.5.

The Schedule should not be treated as merely another ordinary FAR 16.5 multiple-award contract, even though it shares the surface feature of many holders. The governing regime is different: ordering, competition, and pricing under the Schedules follow FAR Subpart 8.4 and GSA's own rules, and the current FAR Part 16 expressly recognizes that the Schedule authorities and rules take precedence over the generic indefinite-delivery procedures.[^c07-part16] This precedence is the practical reason the distinction matters. An action placed under the Schedules is analyzed against the Subpart 8.4 framework, not the Subpart 16.5 framework, so describing a Schedule order as if it were an ordinary FAR 16.5 task order misstates which rules apply. As of June 25, 2026, DoD contracting officers read the Part 16 text in the form rewritten by the Revolutionary FAR Overhaul, applied under a class deviation effective March 16, 2026; the recognition that Schedule rules take precedence is a feature of that overhauled Part 16.[^c07-deviation]

## The three kinds of BPA

A **blanket purchase agreement (BPA)** is a repetitive-purchase arrangement, but "BPA" is not one thing. Three distinct concepts share the abbreviation, and they operate under different authorities and rules:

| BPA type | Authority | What it is |
|---|---|---|
| FAR Part 13 BPA | FAR Part 13 (simplified acquisition) | A simplified "charge account" method for anticipated recurring purchases. |
| Schedule BPA | FAR 8.405-3, established under one or more GSA Schedule contracts | A blanket purchase agreement layered on top of existing Schedule contracts. |
| BPA under another authorized vehicle | The governing vehicle and its applicable rules | A BPA available when the underlying vehicle and the applicable rules permit it. |

A **FAR Part 13 BPA** is the simplified-acquisition "charge account": a method for filling anticipated repetitive needs without writing a separate purchase document each time. A **Schedule BPA** is established under one or more GSA Schedule contracts and is governed by FAR 8.405-3, inheriting the Schedule framework rather than the simplified-acquisition framework.[^c07-bpa] A **BPA under another authorized vehicle** exists only when that vehicle and its applicable rules allow it, and takes its rules from that vehicle. The recurring misconception is to assume that all BPAs operate under the same rules; they do not, and identifying the authority is the first step in any analysis.

Across all three types, the same structural point holds: a BPA generally **establishes a purchasing arrangement**, while the individual **calls or orders** placed against it create the actual purchases. The arrangement is the framework; the calls are the transactions. This is why a BPA is, by itself, frequently not a contract, and why its existence says little about how much has actually been bought — a point connected to the money concepts in [Awards, ceilings, obligations, and spending](03-awards-ceilings-obligations-spending.md) and to the broader catalogue of arrangements that are not contract types in [Common "not a contract type" traps](18-not-a-contract-type-traps.md).

## BOA and basic agreement

Two further instruments are easy to mistake for contracts. A **basic agreement** is a written instrument of understanding that establishes the clauses to apply to future separate contracts entered into during its term. It does not itself contain the work; it pre-positions terms so that later contracts can be executed more efficiently.

A **Basic Ordering Agreement (BOA)** goes one step further. In addition to clauses, it establishes a description of the supplies or services to be provided, methods for pricing, and the mechanics for issuing and delivering against anticipated future orders. The crucial fact is that **neither a basic agreement nor a BOA is itself a contract, and neither obligates the Government to buy anything or promises any future work**.[^c07-far2101] A contract comes into being only when an order is placed against the BOA and the parties agree on its terms.

The most consequential practical implication concerns money. Because a BOA is not a contract and carries no purchase commitment, a BOA "ceiling" is not an awarded contract amount and must not be read as one — it is neither obligated money nor a guarantee of orders. Treating a BOA ceiling as an award is a specific instance of the general "award amount" ambiguity that this reference repeatedly flags; the five distinct dollar figures that hide behind that phrase, and the rule against counting potential ceilings as spending, are developed in [Awards, ceilings, obligations, and spending](03-awards-ceilings-obligations-spending.md). For reporting purposes, BOAs nonetheless surface within the IDV data category alongside Schedules and BPAs, which is exactly why the data-classification-versus-instrument distinction matters: the same record that the data system files under "IDV" may be an instrument that is not, in law, a contract at all.

## Vehicle on-ramps, off-ramps, and access

Holding a vehicle can be effectively binary: a firm that is not a holder generally cannot receive orders under a multiple-award vehicle regardless of its capability, because orders are competed among the existing holder set and a non-holder is simply not in it. An **on-ramp** is the mechanism by which a multiple-award vehicle adds new holders after initial award — through an open season, the creation of a new pool, or a re-solicitation — while an **off-ramp** removes non-performing or ineligible holders, so the holder population is not necessarily fixed for the life of the vehicle.[^c07-onramp] For a non-holder, the realistic routes to participation are limited:

- winning a seat at an on-ramp, when the vehicle opens one;
- qualifying under a different vehicle that already covers the same scope; or
- a teaming or subcontract route under an existing holder.

The full menu of entry choices for a non-holder — including the teaming and subcontract paths — is developed in [Prime–subcontractor structure, privity, and flowdowns](12-prime-contracting-subcontracting-teaming.md), and the question of whether a given vehicle is reachable at all feeds the "addressability" analysis in [Recompetes and opportunity intelligence](05-recompetes-and-opportunity-intelligence.md). A [single-award vehicle](16-single-award-and-sole-source.md) is simply the extreme case: a closed holder set of one, with no on-ramp available to a non-holder at all.

## The throughline

The vehicles and categories in this chapter resolve cleanly once each is assigned to its proper dimension. IDV is a classification used by the federal data systems; IDIQ, GWAC, Schedule, BPA, and BOA are instruments or arrangements, each with its own governing rules — an IDIQ under FAR Subpart 16.5, a Schedule under FAR Subpart 8.4, a Schedule BPA under FAR 8.405-3, a BOA as a non-contract ordering agreement, and a GWAC as an IT-designated, governmentwide IDIQ. The errors this chapter guards against are all the same error wearing different costumes: collapsing the data label into the instrument, or collapsing one instrument's rules into another's. The dimensional discipline that prevents it is set out in [The dimensions of a federal contract](01-dimensions-of-a-federal-contract.md), and the single-award-versus-sole-source distinction that often accompanies these vehicle questions is the subject of [Single-award, single-source, and sole-source](16-single-award-and-sole-source.md).
