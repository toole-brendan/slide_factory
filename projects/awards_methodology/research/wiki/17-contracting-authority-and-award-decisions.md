# The acquisition lifecycle, source selection, and contracting authority

The word **authority** does more work in defense contracting than almost any other, and it names several unrelated concepts that are routinely collapsed into one. A program's *statutory authorization*, an agency's *appropriation*, the budget-process term *contract authority*, an official's delegated power to *bind the Government*, and the power to *place orders* under an existing vehicle are five different things, only some of which are forms of budget authority and only one of which describes the person who signs a contract. This chapter follows an award from the mission need that precedes any solicitation through the source-selection decision and into the post-award protest window; separates the several meanings of "authority"; identifies who actually makes the award decision in the two most common procedures — a FAR Part 15 negotiated source selection and a [FAR Subpart 16.5](06-indefinite-delivery-contracts.md) multiple-award order — describes the supporting cast around the decision; and explains how far the individual decision-maker can be traced from public data. It develops the second analytical warning introduced earlier in this reference: that being within an instrument's [scope](10-period-of-performance-and-scope.md) is necessary but never sufficient, because a lawful contract action must also be executed by an official who holds the authority to take it.

## The pre-award acquisition lifecycle

A contract award is the late, visible terminus of a long internal process, and most of the decisions that determine the eventual award — the requirement itself, the vehicle, any set-aside, the assigned NAICS code, and the evaluation strategy — are settled well before a final solicitation is published. The chain runs from an articulated mission need, through acquisition planning and market research, into a sequence of pre-solicitation exchanges with industry, and only then into the formal solicitation, evaluation, and selection that the public award record reflects. The stages, with their principal regulatory anchors, are these:

- a **mission need** — an articulated operational gap or demand, typically owned by an end user or capability sponsor rather than by a contracting organization;
- a **requirement owner / requiring activity** — the program or organization that converts the need into a defined requirement and sponsors its funding;
- **acquisition planning** under FAR Part 7 — the development of an acquisition strategy and a written acquisition plan that fix, among other things, the contracting approach, competition strategy, and milestones;[^c17-far7]
- **market research** under FAR Part 10 — the analysis of the available industrial base and of commercial capability that informs whether and how to compete the requirement;[^c17-far10]
- a **sources sought notice** or **request for information (RFI)** — a published notice inviting interested firms to identify themselves and their capabilities, often the first public signal of a forthcoming requirement;
- an **industry day** and **one-on-one exchanges** — structured and individual pre-solicitation communications in which the Government describes the emerging requirement and industry responds;
- a **draft solicitation** — a draft request for quotations (RFQ) or request for proposals (RFP) released for comment, exposing the proposed requirement, vehicle, set-aside, NAICS code, and evaluation factors before they are fixed;
- the **final RFQ / RFP** — the binding solicitation against which offers are submitted;
- **evaluation and discussions** — the assessment of offers against the stated factors and, where conducted, exchanges with offerors in the competitive range;
- the **source-selection decision** — the best-value (or other stated-basis) selection of the awardee;
- **award** — the signed contract or order that binds the Government;
- a **debriefing** under FAR 15.506 — the post-award explanation provided to unsuccessful (and, on request, successful) offerors;[^c17-far15506] and
- a **protest** under FAR Subpart 33.1 or before the Government Accountability Office (GAO) — the formal challenge to the conduct or outcome of the procurement.[^c17-far331]

```text
The pre-award acquisition lifecycle (need → award → protest)

  Mission need (end user / sponsor)
        │
  Requirement owner / requiring activity
        │
  Acquisition planning  (FAR Part 7)
        │
  Market research  (FAR Part 10)
        │
  Sources sought / RFI ───────────┐
        │                         │  shaping window:
  Industry day + 1-on-1 exchanges │  requirement, vehicle,
        │                         │  set-aside, NAICS,
  Draft solicitation  ────────────┘  evaluation strategy
        │
  Final RFQ / RFP        ← public solicitation appears here
        │
  Evaluation + discussions
        │
  Source-selection decision  (SSA)
        │
  Award (contracting officer signs)
        │
  Debriefing  (FAR 15.506)
        │
  Protest  (FAR Subpart 33.1 / GAO)
```

The regulatory framing of the early stages is more permissive than is often assumed. FAR 15.201 expressly **encourages** exchanges of information with industry before the receipt of proposals, including presolicitation notices, draft solicitations, requests for information, presolicitation and preproposal conferences, industry days, and one-on-one meetings, so long as the exchanges are consistent with procurement integrity requirements.[^c17-far15201] The analytical consequence is that the leverage available to a prospective offeror is front-loaded. Responding to a sources-sought notice or commenting on a draft solicitation can be more strategically consequential than responding to the final solicitation, because by the time the final RFP issues, the requirement, the contract vehicle, any set-aside, the assigned NAICS code, and the evaluation strategy have usually already been shaped — frequently in direct response to what industry said during the earlier exchanges. The same pre-solicitation notices are the demand signals consumed for opportunity intelligence in [Recompetes and opportunity intelligence from awards data](05-recompetes-and-opportunity-intelligence.md); read forward they are notices an offeror can act on, and read backward they are the published record of how a requirement took shape. Alternative paths — an Other Transaction, a Broad Agency Announcement, or a Commercial Solutions Opening — substitute their own pre-award sequences for parts of this chain, as catalogued in [Alternative acquisition pathways and "not a contract type" traps](18-not-a-contract-type-traps.md).

## Five conditions an award must satisfy

The lifecycle above can be compressed into a neutral analytical model of what must be simultaneously true for a contract award to actually occur. An award is not produced by enthusiasm, by a budget figure, or by a single official's preference; it requires the convergence of five distinct conditions, each owned by a different actor and governed by a different body of rule. The conditions are:

| # | Condition | What it requires | Who supplies it |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | A real end-user requirement | An articulated mission demand, not merely interest | The end user / capability sponsor |
| 2 | An owning organization | A program or requiring activity that holds the requirement | The program office / requiring activity |
| 3 | Available, correct appropriation | Funds that are available and properly chargeable to the requirement | The comptroller / resource manager |
| 4 | A lawful acquisition path | A FAR contract, an order, an Other Transaction, an SBIR Phase III, or a subcontract | The contracting authority |
| 5 | Demonstrable delivery capability | Integration, testing, and schedule sufficient to perform | The offeror |

Several corollaries follow directly, and each corrects a common misreading of how awards arise. A verbal endorsement from an operator satisfies only the first condition; mission interest is necessary but does not by itself create a requirement, a budget, or a contract. A contracting officer cannot manufacture the requirement or the budget — the contracting authority supplies the fourth condition (a lawful path) but neither the demand of the first nor the appropriation of the third. A program manager ordinarily cannot bind the Government; program approval supplies the second condition but not the signature that effects an award, which remains the contracting officer's, as developed below and in [Post-award administration, modifications, and closeout](14-contract-modifications.md). And a budget line does not by itself award a contract; an appropriation satisfies the third condition but must still be joined to a requirement, a lawful path, and a capable performer. The model parallels two distinctions drawn elsewhere in this reference: the split between event-probability and addressability that underlies the two-score recompete model in [Recompetes and opportunity intelligence from awards data](05-recompetes-and-opportunity-intelligence.md), where a likely event and an addressable opportunity are independent facts; and the executable-funding discipline of [CLINs, SLINs, and funding traceability](15-clins-slins-and-funding-traceability.md), where money must be not merely appropriated but available and correctly chargeable on a line item before it can be obligated.

## The several meanings of "authority"

Because the same word is attached to budget law, program law, procurement law, and individual delegations, the first task in reading any contracting statement is to ask *which* authority is meant. The following table separates the senses commonly confused.

| Term | Meaning |
|---|---|
| Contracting officer authority | Delegated authority — usually evidenced by a warrant — to bind the United States within stated limits |
| Ordering authority | Authority granted by a vehicle and a delegation to place task or delivery orders against it |
| Budgetary contract authority | A form of budget authority allowing obligations to be incurred in advance of appropriations |
| Program authorization | Statutory authority for a program, frequently in an authorization act such as the National Defense Authorization Act (NDAA) |
| Appropriation or other budget authority | Legal authority to incur obligations and usually to make payments for specified purposes |
| Awarding/contracting agency | The organization conducting the procurement; not a form of budget authority |

Two distinctions in this table cause the most trouble. The first separates the budget-law sense from the procurement sense of the bare phrase "contract authority." The specialized budget term **contract authority** is a form of *budget authority* that permits an agency to enter into binding obligations in advance of an appropriation, with later **liquidating** appropriations required to pay them.[^c17-contractauthority] It is a feature of how Congress structures spending power, and it is a different concept from the ordinary power of a contracting officer to sign a procurement contract that is already funded. The second distinction separates *ordering authority* from *contracting-officer authority*: the power to place an order against an existing vehicle is conferred by the vehicle and a delegation, and is narrower than, though dependent on, the general warrant power to bind the Government. The two intersect when an ordering contracting officer issues a task order, treated below and in [Indefinite-delivery contracts](06-indefinite-delivery-contracts.md).

### Contracting-officer authority is delegated and bounded

A **contracting officer** may enter into, administer, modify, or terminate contracts, and make related determinations and findings, only to the extent of the authority delegated to that officer (FAR 1.602-1).[^c17-far16021] That delegation is usually evidenced by a **warrant** — a certificate of appointment stating any limits, such as a dollar ceiling or a restriction to particular kinds of actions. The consequence runs throughout this reference: no matter how senior the official or how strongly a program wants a result, only a warranted contracting officer acting within the scope of the warrant can bind the United States. The same rule is why only a contracting officer can sign a [contract modification](14-contract-modifications.md) on the Government's behalf, and why apparent direction from an unwarranted official does not obligate the Government. Authority in this sense is therefore both *delegated* — it flows down from the agency head through appointment — and *bounded* — it stops at the limits the warrant states.

## Who makes the award decision

The phrase "who made the award" has two distinct answers that are easy to conflate: who *selected* the awardee, and who *signed* the resulting contract. The selection decision and the binding signature can rest with different people, and which roles exist at all depends on the acquisition procedure.

### A FAR Part 15 negotiated procurement

In a competitive negotiated procurement under FAR Part 15, the decision is deliberately divided among roles:

- the **evaluation team** evaluates proposals against the solicitation's stated criteria;
- the **Source Selection Authority (SSA)** makes the selection decision — the choice of which proposal represents the best value to the Government;
- the **contracting officer** controls procurement communications with offerors and awards the contract; and
- the contracting officer is the **default SSA** unless another official is appointed to that role.

The selection rests with the SSA, who must exercise *independent judgment* based on the factors and significant subfactors in the solicitation, even when relying on the reports, ratings, and analyses prepared by the evaluation team (FAR 15.303).[^c17-far15303] The SSA's role is thus not to ratify the evaluators' tally but to make a reasoned, documented best-value decision that the evaluation record supports. Separately, the person who *signs* the contract and thereby legally binds the Government is the **authorized contracting officer**. When no separate SSA is appointed, the contracting officer occupies both roles, but the two functions remain conceptually distinct: selecting the awardee and binding the Government are different acts, and in larger acquisitions they are usually performed by different people.

### A FAR 16.5 multiple-award order

The picture changes for an order placed against an existing multiple-award vehicle. For a [FAR Subpart 16.5](06-indefinite-delivery-contracts.md) task or delivery order, the **ordering contracting officer** generally has broad discretion to design streamlined order-placement procedures appropriate to the requirement.[^c17-overhaul16] The formal source-selection procedures of FAR Subpart 15.3 — including the appointment of an SSA and the apparatus of a full source-selection plan — do not automatically apply to a fair-opportunity order competition, although the vehicle's terms or the individual order solicitation may *adopt* similar procedures by choice. In other words, the multiple-award structure was already established when the parent vehicle was awarded; placing an order draws on the ordering authority the parent created rather than reopening a Part 15 selection, so the decision-maker for an order is ordinarily the ordering contracting officer exercising fair-opportunity discretion rather than a separately appointed SSA. The codified-versus-deviation citation note is material here: under the current DoD class deviation adopting the Revolutionary FAR Overhaul, fair-opportunity procedures sit primarily at FAR 16.507, whereas still-codified FAR places many of them at FAR 16.505.[^c17-overhaul16]

## The supporting roles

A source selection or an order decision is the product of many participants, only two of whom can actually decide the award or bind the Government. The following table sets out the typical division of labor.

| Participant | Typical role |
|---|---|
| Program or requiring office | Defines the need and the technical requirement |
| Technical evaluation team | Evaluates technical submissions |
| Cost/price analysts | Assess price, cost, and realism where applicable |
| Legal counsel | Advises on process, scope, protests, and documentation |
| Small-business specialist / SBA | Advises or acts on set-aside and program issues |
| Resource manager | Certifies that funds are available and properly chargeable |
| Source Selection Authority | Makes the source-selection decision when that role applies |
| Contracting officer | Executes the award and binds the Government |

The resource manager's certification connects directly to the funding dimension: an award cannot be obligated unless funds are available and properly chargeable to the requirement, the discipline traced through [CLINs, SLINs, and funding traceability](15-clins-slins-and-funding-traceability.md). The small-business specialist's role connects to the set-aside and program decisions described in [Single-award, single-source, and sole-source](16-single-award-and-sole-source.md). A common error is to assume that the most senior or most visible official is the one who decided the award. A **program manager**, a **program executive officer (PEO)**, or a **Milestone Decision Authority** may approve the underlying program or its acquisition strategy, but that approval does not by itself make any of those officials the SSA or the contracting officer. The acquisition-strategy approval and the source-selection decision are different acts performed under different authorities, and the signature that binds the Government remains the contracting officer's.

## What public data shows about competition

Because the published solicitation sits late in the lifecycle, the public record systematically understates how much of a competition was already settled before any document appeared on a contract-opportunities portal. Three properties of the available public data follow from this timing, and each bounds what an analyst can responsibly infer about competition.

First, the **published solicitation is relatively late**. By the time a final RFQ or RFP is visible, the shaping decisions of the pre-award lifecycle — requirement, vehicle, set-aside, NAICS code, and evaluation strategy — are largely fixed, so the solicitation reveals the *outcome* of the shaping window rather than the deliberations within it. The earlier sources-sought notices, RFIs, and draft solicitations are the more revealing artifacts, which is why they are treated as demand signals in [Recompetes and opportunity intelligence from awards data](05-recompetes-and-opportunity-intelligence.md).

Second, **forecasts and long-range estimates are nonbinding planning clues**. Agency acquisition forecasts and the long-range acquisition estimates contemplated by FAR 5.404 are explicitly planning information; they signal anticipated requirements but commit the Government to nothing, and a forecast entry is not a solicitation, a funded requirement, or a promise to compete.[^c17-forecast] An analyst can read them as evidence of intent and approximate timing, not as evidence that an award will occur as described or on the stated schedule.

Third, **CPARS is controlled and not public**. The Contractor Performance Assessment Reporting System (CPARS) — the Government's record of contractor past performance — is source-selection-sensitive and access-controlled, not a public dataset, so the performance history that materially informs many best-value decisions cannot be reconstructed from open sources.[^c17-cpars] CPARS is the past-performance feed described in [Post-award administration, modifications, and closeout](14-contract-modifications.md), and its controlled status is a principal reason the evidentiary basis for a selection is harder to see than the fact of the award.

These three limits sharpen the same evidentiary asymmetry developed below for the decision-maker: the public record is reliable about *organizations and the fact of an award* and unreliable about the *deliberative inputs and the named individuals* behind it. The awarding and funding office are traceable from public data in a way the individual decision-maker, the source-selection rationale, and the underlying past-performance record are not.

## How far the decision-maker can be traced publicly

The recurring theme of this reference — that public data trace the *account and office* far better than the *exact budget line and the named individual* — applies sharply to authority. From a signed contract or [modification](14-contract-modifications.md) and the federal data systems, one can usually identify the **awarding agency**, the **awarding office**, the **funding agency**, the **funding office**, and sometimes the **contracting officer** named on the instrument. What is generally *not* a standard field is the identity of the SSA or of the individual evaluators: that information is not carried in USAspending and instead may surface only in a **source-selection decision document**, a **debriefing**, a **GAO or court protest record**, the **contract file**, a **congressional record**, or a **FOIA production**.[^c17-usaspending]

The cleanest way to state the limit is comparative. Public data usually let an analyst trace the **funding account** better than the exact budget line, and the **awarding office** better than the individual decision-maker. Recovering an exact Program Element or budget-line item, or attaching a name to the source-selection decision, normally requires the underlying procurement and accounting documents rather than the public award record. This is the same evidentiary asymmetry drawn for budget lines in [CLINs, SLINs, and funding traceability](15-clins-slins-and-funding-traceability.md): the structures the data systems are designed to report — organizations, accounts, transactions — are visible, while the structures they are not designed to report — internal deliberative roles and the finest budget granularity — generally are not. For the reader of a contract announcement, the practical rule is to treat the awarding office and funding account as knowable from public data, and to treat the named SSA and the exact budget line as facts that ordinarily must be sourced from the procurement file itself.
